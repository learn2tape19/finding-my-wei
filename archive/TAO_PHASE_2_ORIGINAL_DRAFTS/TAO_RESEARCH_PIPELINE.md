# THE TAO OF CLINICAL TOUCH — Research Pipeline

**Status:** 10-year research agenda  
**Planning Horizon:** 2026-2036  
**Authority:** Drew Freedman  
**Purpose:** Translate Tao principles into testable research questions

---

# Research Philosophy

Research in permission-based practice serves these purposes:

1. **Validate Core Claims** — Does permission actually matter? Can it be reliably assessed?
2. **Measure Effectiveness** — Do permission-focused practitioners achieve better outcomes?
3. **Understand Mechanisms** — How does permission create change? What nervous system markers indicate it?
4. **Guide Practice** — What assessment protocols work best? When should practitioners refer?
5. **Build Field** — Contribute to knowledge that advances manual therapy as a profession
6. **Enable Teaching** — Give educators and students evidence-based frameworks

This is not defensive research (proving we're right). It's curious research (understanding how healing works).

# PRIORITY 1: Permission as Measurable Construct (2026-2028)

## Research Question 1.1: Can Permission Be Reliably Assessed?

**Goal:** Develop and validate a permission assessment instrument

**Rationale:** Difficult to teach what you can't measure. Alliance has validated assessment tools; permission needs them.

**Research Team:**
- Drew Freedman (co-investigator, clinical advisor)
- University partner (methodology, data analysis)
- Practitioner network (data collection)

**Design:**
- Qualitative phase: In-depth interviews with experienced practitioners (n=15-20)
  - How do you recognize permission in a session?
  - What blocks permission?
  - How do you know when you've created it?
- Expert consensus: Develop preliminary assessment dimensions
- Pilot quantitative: Develop and test assessment tool (n=50-100 practitioners)
- Validation study: Multi-site validation (n=200-300)

**Outcomes:**
- Permission Assessment Scale (PAS) — validated 5-10 item instrument
- Clinical decision tree (when to assess permission)
- Practitioner training protocol for consistent assessment
- Publication: "Development and Validation of a Permission Assessment Scale for Manual Therapy"

**Timeline:** 
- Year 1: Qualitative phase + expert consensus
- Year 2: Pilot + validation study
- Year 3: Final publication + training protocol

## Research Question 1.2: What Nervous System Markers Indicate Permission?

**Goal:** Identify measurable physiological indicators of permission

**Rationale:** If permission is nervous system state, what does healthy permission look like physiologically?

**Research Team:**
- Drew Freedman (clinical advisor)
- Neuroscience collaborator (physiology expertise)
- University partner (instrumentation)

**Design:**
- Literature review: Nervous system markers in allied healthcare
- Preliminary exploration: Measure heart rate variability, respiratory rate, skin conductance in sessions with/without permission
- Hypothesis development: Which markers correlate with subjective permission
- Pilot study: Measure multiple markers during standardized sessions (n=20-30)
- Validation: Larger study measuring markers during clinical work (n=50-100)

**Possible Markers:**
- Heart rate variability (HRV) — higher variability suggests better regulation
- Respiratory coherence — synchronized breathing patterns
- Skin conductance — lower activation suggests safety
- Postural openness — less protective positioning
- Vocal patterns — lower vocal tension, slower speech
- Eye contact patterns — more available gaze

**Outcomes:**
- Identified nervous system signature of permission
- Biofeedback protocols practitioners could learn to recognize
- Assessment tools for practitioners
- Research foundation for clinical decision-making
- Publication: "Physiological Markers of Permission in Manual Therapy Sessions"

**Timeline:**
- Year 1: Literature review + preliminary exploration
- Year 2: Pilot study + marker identification
- Year 3: Validation study + practitioner protocols

# PRIORITY 2: Alliance & Outcomes (2027-2029)

## Research Question 2.1: Does Alliance Predict Better Outcomes in Manual Therapy?

**Goal:** First comparative outcomes study in manual therapy alliance research

**Rationale:** Meta-analyses show alliance > technique in psychotherapy. Does this hold for manual therapy?

**Research Team:**
- Drew Freedman (clinical advisor)
- Outcomes researcher (methodology)
- Practitioner network (recruitment + data collection)
- University partner (statistical analysis)

**Design:**
- Prospective cohort: Recruit clients from permission-trained practitioners (n=100-150)
- Measure:
  - Alliance at session 1 (validated working alliance inventory)
  - Technique skills (independent rater observed sessions)
  - Client outcomes (pain, function, satisfaction, return rate)
- Analysis:
  - Regression model: Does alliance predict outcomes better than technique skill?
  - Mediation analysis: How does alliance create better outcomes?

**Outcome Measures:**
- Pain reduction (0-10 scale)
- Functional improvement (specific to client's goal)
- Session satisfaction
- Return rate (% who continue care)
- Perceived benefit (did therapy help?)
- Practice satisfaction (would refer again?)

**Outcomes:**
- Evidence that alliance predicts manual therapy outcomes
- Quantified effect size (how much does alliance matter?)
- Practitioner confidence in alliance-first approach
- Business case for training (practitioners with better alliance have better retention)
- Publication: "Therapeutic Alliance as Predictor of Outcomes in Manual Therapy"

**Timeline:**
- Year 1-2: Recruitment + data collection
- Year 2-3: Analysis + publication

**Sample Size & Power:** n=100-150 gives sufficient power to detect alliance effect

## Research Question 2.2: Can Alliance Training Improve Outcomes?

**Goal:** Randomized trial comparing traditional vs. alliance-focused training

**Rationale:** If alliance matters, does training in alliance-building improve outcomes?

**Research Team:**
- Drew Freedman (curriculum co-design)
- Outcomes researcher (trial design)
- Two training programs (one teaching alliance, one traditional)
- University partner (randomization + analysis)

**Design:**
- Randomized controlled trial of practitioner training
- Intervention: Alliance-focused Level II training (intervention group) vs. traditional continuing education (control group)
- Randomize: 40-60 practitioners per group
- Measure clients:
  - Outcomes for clients seen by intervention vs. control practitioners
  - Alliance quality (working alliance inventory)
  - Satisfaction, pain, function, retention

**Primary Outcome:** Client outcomes differ between groups (clients of alliance-trained practitioners do better)

**Secondary Outcomes:**
- Alliance scores differ
- Practitioner confidence improves
- Practitioner retention improves
- Client satisfaction higher

**Outcomes:**
- Definitive evidence that training works
- Curriculum validated through RCT
- Market differentiator (evidence-based training)
- Justification for scale investment
- Publication: "Randomized Trial of Alliance-Focused Training in Manual Therapy"

**Timeline:**
- Year 1: Trial design + recruitment
- Year 2-3: Data collection
- Year 3-4: Analysis + publication

**Sample Size:** 80-120 practitioners (40-60 per group) with ~10-15 clients each = 800-1,800 client outcomes

# PRIORITY 3: Permission in Specific Populations (2027-2031)

## Research Question 3.1: Permission in Trauma Survivors

**Goal:** How does permission-based approach change outcomes for trauma clients?

**Rationale:** Trauma dramatically affects nervous system trust; permission framework should help. Need evidence.

**Research Team:**
- Drew Freedman (clinical advisor)
- Trauma researcher (expertise)
- Practitioners working with trauma populations

**Design:**
- Case series: 10-15 detailed case reports of trauma clients
- Document:
  - Client history and trauma response
  - Permission assessment
  - Clinical decisions based on permission state
  - Outcomes (symptom reduction, trust, autonomy)
- Analyze: Common patterns in permission-based trauma care

**Outcomes:**
- Deep understanding of trauma + permission
- Case reports for publication
- Teaching materials for trauma-specific training
- Practitioner confidence in trauma work
- Publications: Case reports + review article

**Timeline:** 2 years

---

## Research Question 3.2: Permission in Chronic Pain

**Goal:** Can permission-first approach improve outcomes in chronic pain clients?

**Rationale:** Chronic pain involves nervous system fear/protection; permission framework targets this.

**Research Design:**
- Prospective cohort: 50-80 clients with chronic pain
- Measure:
  - Pain (baseline, 3, 6, 12 months)
  - Fear of movement (Tampa Scale)
  - Permission and safety assessment
  - Function and quality of life
  - Practice satisfaction

**Outcomes:**
- Data showing pain + permission relationship
- Evidence that safety-first approach improves outcomes
- Rationale for why practitioners fail with pain (ignore nervous system)
- Publications + teaching materials

**Timeline:** 2-3 years

# PRIORITY 4: Teaching & Learning (2028-2032)

## Research Question 4.1: Does Permission-Based Teaching Produce Different Practitioners?

**Goal:** Compare practitioners trained with vs. without permission focus

**Rationale:** Book says practitioners learn based on how they're taught. Test this.

**Research Design:**
- Survey + interview: 100+ practitioners with different training backgrounds
- Measure:
  - Teaching they received (permission-focused vs. technique-focused)
  - Their current practice style
  - Client outcomes they see
  - Job satisfaction, burnout
  - Retention (still practicing?)

**Outcomes:**
- Evidence that training style predicts practice style
- Link between permission-based teaching and better outcomes
- Justification for curriculum investment
- Publications

**Timeline:** 2-3 years

---

## Research Question 4.2: What Makes Permission-Based Teaching Work?

**Goal:** Identify key teaching elements that create permission-based practitioners

**Rationale:** We have theory; now identify what actually changes how students think.

**Design:**
- Qualitative study: Observe 5-10 excellent permission-based teachers
- Document:
  - What they do that creates permission
  - How students respond
  - What sticks with students
- Interview: Students 6-12 months after training
- Identify: Key elements that predict student transformation

**Outcomes:**
- Teacher training protocols
- What matters most in education
- Ability to assess teacher effectiveness
- Publications

**Timeline:** 2 years

# PRIORITY 5: Mechanism Studies (2029-2033)

## Research Question 5.1: How Does Permission Create Healing?

**Goal:** Mechanistic study of permission → outcomes pathway

**Rationale:** We know (from priorities 2-3) that permission predicts outcomes. But *how*?

**Design:**
- Detailed process study: 20-30 video-recorded sessions
- Measure:
  - Permission-building moments (identified by raters)
  - Client nervous system response (HRV, skin conductance)
  - Client subjective response (post-session interview)
  - Outcome trajectory
- Analyze: What moments create change? What nervous system patterns emerge?

**Outcomes:**
- Understanding of healing mechanism
- Video examples of permission-driven change
- Teaching materials showing mechanism
- Research advancing understanding
- Publications

**Timeline:** 2-3 years

---

## Research Question 5.2: Practitioner Nervous System & Client Outcomes

**Goal:** Does practitioner's own nervous system regulation affect client outcomes?

**Rationale:** Alliance involves two nervous systems. Does practitioner regulation matter?

**Design:**
- Measure:
  - Practitioner nervous system state (HRV, breathing, presence)
  - Client outcomes
  - Alliance quality
- Analysis: Does practitioner regulation predict client outcomes?

**Outcomes:**
- Evidence that practitioner self-regulation matters
- Justification for ongoing self-care + training
- Understanding of co-regulation in sessions
- Publications

**Timeline:** 2-3 years

# PRIORITY 6: Comparative & Health Economics (2030-2034)

## Research Question 6.1: Permission-Based vs. Traditional: Cost-Effectiveness

**Goal:** Compare healthcare outcomes and costs across training approaches

**Rationale:** If permission-based care is better, healthcare systems should know.

**Design:**
- Database study: Track 500+ clients across different practitioner training backgrounds
- Measure:
  - Direct costs (treatment time, number of sessions)
  - Health outcomes (pain, function, satisfaction)
  - Downstream healthcare use (referrals, additional providers)
  - Client satisfaction and retention

**Outcomes:**
- Cost-effectiveness evidence
- Justification for health system investment in training
- Reimbursement rationale
- Business case for education programs
- Publications in health economics journals

**Timeline:** 3-4 years

## Research Question 6.2: Scaling Permission-Based Practice

**Goal:** What does it take to train 100+ practitioners in a region?

**Rationale:** Learn from real-world implementation. What works? What doesn't?

**Design:**
- Implementation science approach
- Document: Barriers and facilitators to scaling

**Outcomes:**
- Implementation protocols for other regions
- Scaling guidance for field
- Publications
