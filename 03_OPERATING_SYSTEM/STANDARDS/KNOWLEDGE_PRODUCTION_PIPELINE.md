# Knowledge Production Pipeline

**Version:** 2.0  
**Effective Date:** July 9, 2026  
**Scope:** All intellectual work across Finding My Wei, Project Atlas, and all subsidiary domains  
**Governance:** Institutional standard (organizational level, not departmental)

---

## Governing Principle

**Create knowledge once. Publish it everywhere with purpose. Learn from every publication.**

This principle captures the entire lifecycle:
- **Create once** → Avoid duplicate intellectual effort; wisdom is produced once, then distributed
- **Publish everywhere** → Maximize reach and impact while preserving consistency across channels
- **With purpose** → Every platform has a distinct role; publishing is strategic, not just replication
- **Learn from every publication** → The institution continuously improves based on what resonates, what questions emerge, what insights the audience provides

## Phase 1: Question / Investigation

**Trigger:** 
- Research question from Project Atlas
- Gap identified in teaching materials (Learn2Tape)
- Customer question that reveals pattern (Sidekick Air)
- Author's emerging insight (The Tao)
- Audience question in comments/emails

**Deliverable:** `INVESTIGATION.md`

Document:
- What is the question we're investigating?
- Why does this matter to our audience?
- What context makes this urgent now?
- What perspective are we bringing that's different?
- What would constitute a complete answer?

---

## Phase 2: Research

**Scope:** Gather sources, evidence, examples, data

**Deliverable:** `RESEARCH.md` (evidence folder)

Collect:
- Academic/clinical sources
- Our own data (student work, client outcomes, feedback)
- Expert quotes or frameworks
- Contrary evidence (what might challenge our hypothesis?)
- Examples that illuminate the concept

---

## Phase 3: Editorial Thesis

**This is where thinking crystallizes before writing begins.**

**Deliverable:** `EDITORIAL_THESIS.md`

The thesis is a 200–300 word document that captures:

### The Central Claim
State in one sentence what the article will argue. This is *not* the headline. It's the underlying truth the article defends.

*Example: "Informed consent and nervous system permission are physiologically distinct states, and clinicians must deliberately earn permission before intervention."*

### The Supporting Arguments (3–5 points)
Each argument is 1–2 sentences. These become the article's section headings.

1. [Core argument 1 stated concisely]
2. [Core argument 2 stated concisely]
3. [etc.]

### Why This Matters (to the audience)
1–2 sentences: Who needs to understand this? What changes if they do?

### What We're Not Arguing
Sometimes clarity requires stating what you're *not* saying. This prevents misinterpretation.

*Example: "We're not arguing that informed consent is unnecessary. We're arguing it's insufficient without nervous system permission."*

### New Questions This Raises
List 2–3 questions that emerge from this thesis. These become seeds for future articles and Project Atlas investigations.

## Phase 4: Editorial Brief

**Deliverable:** `EDITORIAL_BRIEF.md`

(Described in detail in the Canonical Publication Workflow v1.0)

The brief takes the thesis and specifies:
- Target audience
- Tone and voice
- Word count target
- Opening strategy
- Structure/sections
- Key elements to extract (for later platforms)

---

## Phase 5: Canonical Publication (Blog)

**Deliverable:** `BLOG/[DAY].md`

Requirements:
- 700–1,200 words (unless research requires different scope)
- Opening hook or vignette
- Arguments developed with evidence/examples
- Practical takeaway
- Call to action
- Attribution for sources
- Ready for direct WordPress publication

**This is the source document.** Everything downstream traces here.

---

## Phase 6: Editorial Extraction

**Deliverable:** `EDITORIAL_EXTRACTION.md`

Before creating platform-specific content, extract:
- Strongest opening hook
- Key insight (the thesis, restated for social)
- Best quote
- Practical takeaway
- Call to action
- Visual metaphor
- SEO keywords (primary + secondary)
- Platform-specific hooks (how does this work on each channel?)

---

## Phase 7: Creative Direction

**Deliverable:** `CREATIVE_DIRECTION.md`

Specifies all visual derivatives:
- Hero image concept (from visual metaphor)
- Carousel concepts (one per key insight)
- Story concept
- Pull quote graphics
- Thumbnails
- Adobe Express specifications
- Brand color + typography
- Accessibility standards (contrast, alt text, readability)

## Phase 10: Measurement

**Deliverable:** `MEASUREMENT.md`

Set up measurement *before* publication:

### Website Metrics
- Page views (unique + repeat)
- Time on page
- Scroll depth (did people read the whole article?)
- Click-through rate to related articles
- Bounce rate

### Social Metrics
- Impressions (reach)
- Engagement rate (likes + comments + shares)
- Click-through rate to article
- Top-performing post variant (if testing)
- Follower growth during campaign

### Email Metrics
- Open rate
- Click-through rate
- Unsubscribe rate
- Forward rate (audience sharing)

### Audience Metrics
- New comments (quantity + content)
- Reader questions (what are people asking?)
- Shares/citations (who's amplifying this?)
- Private feedback (emails, DMs)

### Conversion Metrics
- Did this article lead to inquiries? (Sidekick Air)
- Did this article lead to course signups? (Learn2Tape)
- Did this article lead to Project Atlas investigations?

---

## Phase 11: Institutional Learning

**Deliverable:** `INSTITUTIONAL_LEARNING.md`

This is where the loop closes. Capture:

### Hypothesis vs. Reality
- **What was the thesis we were testing?** (from Editorial Thesis)
- **What actually resonated?** (from comments, shares, questions)
- **What surprised us?** (unexpected angle people engaged with)
- **What fell flat?** (argument we thought was important; audience didn't care)

### Content Performance
- **Which headline performed best?** (if testing variants)
- **Which section generated the most questions?** (scroll depth + comments)
- **Which image generated the most engagement?** (platform analytics)
- **Which platform drove the most website traffic?** (attribution)

### Audience Insights
- **What questions did readers ask?** (in comments, emails)
- **What objections emerged?** (contrary points readers raised)
- **Which audience segment engaged most?** (demographics from analytics)
- **What new perspectives did readers contribute?**

### Research Implications
- **What new research questions emerged?** (feed these to Project Atlas)
- **Should we investigate further?** (is there a follow-up article?)
- **Did this connect unexpected dots?** (new connections to existing work)
- **What contradictions appeared?** (evidence that challenges our thesis)

### Institutional Actions
- **Should Project Atlas investigate further?** (if yes, write investigation brief)
- **Should we create a follow-up article?** (if questions warrant deeper exploration)
- **Should we update existing articles?** (if new evidence contradicts prior work)
- **Should we correct anything?** (did we make an error that needs fixing?)
- **Should we change our thesis for future articles?** (did this publication reveal a gap in our thinking?)

### Feedback Loop
Document what you learned and how it will shape the next publication in this series (or a new series).

## Governance

### Roles

**Research & Editorial** (responsible for Phases 1–5)
- Owns the thesis and argument
- Ensures article quality, research accuracy
- Signs off on blog publication before moving to extraction

**Platform & Creative** (responsible for Phases 6–9)
- Adapts editorial work for each platform
- Ensures visual consistency, brand alignment
- Manages deployment and scheduling

**Analytics & Measurement** (responsible for Phases 10–11)
- Sets up measurement before publication
- Tracks performance during publication window (typically 2 weeks)
- Compiles institutional learning report
- Recommends next investigation or follow-up article

### Workflow Gates

- **Gate 1 (After Phase 3):** Editorial Thesis review. Does the central claim hold? Are supporting arguments sound?
- **Gate 2 (After Phase 5):** Blog publication review. Does the article deliver on the thesis? Is it ready for WordPress?
- **Gate 3 (After Phase 9):** Deployment review. Is everything scheduled, formatted, tracked correctly?
- **Gate 4 (After Phase 11):** Institutional Learning review. Does the learning inform the next investigation?

## Why This Matters

### For The Tao of Clinical Touch
Every article published feeds into CE curriculum design, book revisions, speaking topics. The publication process becomes research.

### For Project Atlas
Every publication from other domains feeds research questions back into Project Atlas. The blog becomes the institution's thinking made public, which surfaces gaps worth investigating.

### For Learn2Tape
Every article published tests which concepts resonate, which questions emerge, which audience segments engage. This informs curriculum updates and new course design.

### For Sidekick Air / StitchCore
Every technical article published reveals customer concerns, clarifies positioning, tests market messaging. The blog becomes R&D feedback.

### For Finding My Wei as a Whole
Over hundreds of articles, this feedback loop becomes competitive advantage. Your institutional knowledge compounds. Your thinking publicly evolves. Your audience sees you learning in real time.

## Principles

1. **Think before writing.** Editorial Thesis clarifies thinking. This prevents wasted effort on articles that don't deliver insight.

2. **Write once, publish everywhere with purpose.** The blog is the source. Every other platform is a strategic adaptation, not a repost.

3. **Measure what matters.** Before publication, define what success looks like. Track it. Learn from it.

4. **Close the loop.** Every publication must feed information back into the institution. No dead ends.

5. **Enduring over viral.** A blog article that drives traffic for years compounds. A social post that goes viral for a week is entertainment. This pipeline produces enduring assets.

6. **Audience as collaborators.** Comments, questions, and feedback are research data. Treat them as such.

7. **Transparency builds trust.** When your audience sees you learning, correcting, investigating further—trust deepens.
