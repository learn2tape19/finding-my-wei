# Governance Principle: Evidence-Based Revision

**Established:** July 9, 2026  
**Status:** Constitutional Rule (binding on all future work)

---

## The Rule

**No standard, workflow, or constitutional document may be revised without evidence from execution demonstrating that the current standard materially limits the institution's effectiveness.**

---

## What This Means

### Revision Requires:
1. **Documented problem** — Specific friction point from real work, not theoretical concern
2. **Materiality test** — The problem meaningfully limits institutional effectiveness, not just convenience
3. **Evidence** — Data from actual execution, not speculation
4. **Documented impact** — Clear record of what the limitation prevented

### What Does NOT Trigger Revision:
- "I think this could be improved"
- "This would be more elegant if..."
- "I noticed a small inefficiency"
- Theoretical optimization
- Preference for different phrasing
- Anything without evidence from execution

---

## Why This Rule Exists

### The Problem It Prevents

Institutions decay through endless redesign. Teams spend more time improving processes than executing them. Standards become unstable. Knowledge gets lost as frameworks change.

### The Antidote

Lock standards. Execute work. Gather evidence. Only then revise.

This creates:
- **Stability** — Standards remain consistent long enough to reveal whether they work
- **Institutional memory** — Knowledge doesn't scatter with each redesign
- **Evidence discipline** — Changes are justified by reality, not preference
- **Execution priority** — Time goes to work, not process optimization

---

## The Burden of Proof

If you believe a standard should change, you must document:

1. **What is the current standard?** (Quote it exactly)
2. **What problem did you encounter?** (Describe specific friction from execution)
3. **How did it limit effectiveness?** (What became impossible or unnecessarily difficult?)
4. **What evidence do you have?** (Screenshots, timestamps, failed attempts, feedback)
5. **What would you change?** (Specific revision proposed)
6. **Why would this fix the problem?** (Logical connection between change and resolution)

---

## The Review Process

When a revision is proposed:

1. **Validation** — Is the evidence genuine? Did the limitation actually occur?
2. **Materiality** — Did this materially limit the institution, or just create friction?
3. **Scope** — Is the proposed change the minimal fix, or does it require broader redesign?
4. **Testing** — If approved, is the revision tested before it becomes constitutional?
5. **Documentation** — Is the decision recorded with the evidence that prompted it?

## Examples

### REJECTED (No Evidence)
"I think the Editorial Extraction phase could be combined with the Editorial Brief to save time."

**Why rejected:** Preference, not evidence. No proof that these phases actually need combining.

---

### REJECTED (Insufficient Materiality)
"The Publication Success Criteria scoring system is slightly confusing because the 0–3 scale could be 0–5 instead."

**Why rejected:** Confusion about scoring is minor. No evidence that it limited institutional effectiveness. No failed publication because of this.

---

### CONSIDERED (Potential Evidence)

"After publishing 6 articles using the full pipeline, we've noticed that the Editorial Extraction phase regularly takes 6+ hours. This is slowing publication schedules. Three different editors have mentioned this friction independently. We propose combining Extraction with the Blog review phase to reduce handoffs."

**Status:** Evidence warrants review. Needs data (time logs, publication schedule impact) to proceed.

---

### APPROVED (Strong Evidence)

"We've published 12 articles. In 11 of them, the Editorial Extraction phase identified the same five elements (hook, insight, quote, takeaway, CTA) in the same order. The extraction document is now 85% boilerplate. We propose: replace the document with a template that teams complete in 30 minutes instead of 6 hours. We have time logs showing this would reduce extraction time by 70% without losing quality (verified by comparing template output to current process output for last 5 articles)."

**Status:** Approved. Revision is minimal, evidence is clear, materiality is proven, testing is documented.

## This Rule Applies To

- Constitutional documents (Knowledge Lifecycle, etc.)
- Operational standards (Knowledge Production Pipeline, etc.)
- Success criteria (Publication Success Criteria, etc.)
- Workflows and processes
- Governance principles (including this one)

---

## Exception: Correction vs. Revision

A **correction** (fixing an error) does not require evidence of material limitation.

Examples of corrections:
- "The article says Phase 4 comes after Phase 3, but the diagram shows Phase 4 before Phase 3" (factual error)
- "The workflow document references a file path that doesn't exist" (technical error)
- "The success criteria includes a typo" (editing error)

Corrections can be made immediately and documented.

**Revisions** (changing meaning, scope, or approach) require the evidence burden of proof.

---

## Annual Review

Once per year, the governance principle itself is reviewed:

1. **Is this rule serving the institution?** Does it prevent endless redesign without creating harmful rigidity?
2. **Are standards stable enough?** Or are they too rigid, blocking necessary evolution?
3. **Is the evidence burden too high?** Or too low?

This annual review is the ONLY scheduled opportunity to reconsider the rule itself.

---

## Final Note

This rule exists because institutional knowledge compounds through **consistency and practice**, not through **optimization and redesign**.

The best process, rigorously executed 100 times, produces better results than the perfect process executed twice and then abandoned.

Lock it. Use it. Learn from it. Only change it when evidence demands it.
