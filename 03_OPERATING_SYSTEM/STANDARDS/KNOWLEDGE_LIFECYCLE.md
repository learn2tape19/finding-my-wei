**Version:** 1.0  
**Effective Date:** July 9, 2026

## Purpose

This document defines the lifecycle of knowledge within Finding My Wei. It explains *why* the Knowledge Production Pipeline, Editorial Standards, Creative Standards, and all supporting workflows exist.

Knowledge moves through eight stages. Each stage has a permanent artifact. Each stage serves a specific purpose in the institutional mission.

---

## The Eight Stages

### Stage 1: Curiosity
**Purpose:** Identify meaningful questions worth investigating  
**Permanent Artifact:** Investigation Brief  
**Outputs:** Research question, scope, relevance to institutional mission  

When a pattern emerges, a customer question recurs, or a gap appears in our thinking, it becomes an Investigation. This stage asks: *Is this worth our time? Does it matter to our mission?*

**Example:** "Why do some patients respond dramatically to permission-earning while others remain defended despite it?" → Investigation Brief

---

### Stage 2: Research
**Purpose:** Reduce uncertainty through evidence gathering  
**Permanent Artifact:** Research Summary  
**Outputs:** Sources collected, evidence evaluated, contrary arguments documented  

Research is not opinion-building. It's uncertainty reduction. We gather evidence, study existing frameworks, interview practitioners, analyze data. We explicitly document what contradicts our hypothesis, not just what confirms it.

**Example:** Literature review on polyvagal theory, nervous system defense, clinical outcomes with regulated practitioners

---

### Stage 3: Synthesis
**Purpose:** Develop a coherent position from disparate sources  
**Permanent Artifact:** Editorial Thesis  
**Outputs:** Central claim, supporting arguments, implications, new questions raised  

This is where thinking crystallizes. We take the research and synthesize it into a position we can defend. The Editorial Thesis is our first written artifact because it represents the first moment when we commit to an argument.

If synthesis reveals the research doesn't support our hypothesis, we acknowledge it. The thesis may conclude: "This isn't ready yet" or "The answer is different than we thought."

**Example:** "Permission is physiologically distinct from informed consent, and can be deliberately earned through five specific clinician behaviors"

---

### Stage 4: Publication
**Purpose:** Share knowledge publicly in canonical form  
**Permanent Artifact:** Canonical Blog Article  
**Outputs:** 700–1,200 word article that defends the thesis with evidence and examples  

Publication is not promotion. It's the institutional commitment to an idea. The blog article is permanent. It carries our name. It will be read years from now.

This is why the article must be rigorously edited, factually grounded, and honestly argued. We're not selling. We're publishing.

**Example:** "The Permission Question: Before You Touch Anything, Ask Yourself This" (847 words)

---

### Stage 5: Distribution
**Purpose:** Reach different audiences through channel-appropriate adaptation  
**Permanent Artifact:** Platform Adaptations  
**Outputs:** Facebook posts, Instagram carousels, LinkedIn insights, email teasers, Threads conversations

The blog reaches one audience with one format. Distribution recognizes that different people consume knowledge differently. A busy LinkedIn professional may never read a full blog article. An Instagram follower may engage with a carousel that a blog reader would ignore.

Distribution is strategic. Every platform adaptation traces back to the canonical article. We're not creating seven different ideas; we're adapting one idea to seven contexts.

**Example:** Same core insight ("permission is distinct from consent") appears as:
- LinkedIn: Professional/leadership angle
- Instagram: Visual/carousel format
- Facebook: Community discussion prompt
- Email: Teaser + link to full article

---

### Stage 6: Observation
**Purpose:** Measure institutional impact and audience response  
**Permanent Artifact:** Analytics & Measurement Report  
**Outputs:** Performance data, reader questions, engagement patterns, platform effectiveness  

Observation is not vanity metrics. We're measuring:
- Did the hypothesis reach readers clearly?
- Which sections generated the most interest?
- What questions emerged from readers?
- Which platform drove the most meaningful engagement?
- Did this contribute to long-term website authority?

**Example:** "Permission" article reached 2,100 readers; generated 47 comments; top question was "What about high-volume settings?"

---

### Stage 7: Reflection
**Purpose:** Capture lessons and extract institutional learning  
**Permanent Artifact:** Institutional Learning Report  
**Outputs:** What resonated, what surprised us, what contradicted our thesis, new questions for research  

Reflection is disciplined analysis. We don't just collect analytics; we ask:
- What assumptions did our thesis make that readers challenged?
- Which audience segment engaged most? Why?
- What new questions did readers raise?
- Did we make errors we should correct?
- Should we investigate further?

**Example:** Readers understood permission intuitively but had no professional language for it. The article gave them framework for existing practice, not new technique.

---

### Stage 8: Integration
**Purpose:** Update institutional knowledge and feed new research  
**Permanent Artifact:** Standards Updates & Project Atlas Investigations  
**Outputs:** Refinements to editorial standards, new investigations for Project Atlas, curriculum updates, strategic recommendations  

Integration closes the loop. Learning from one publication informs the next investigation. Patterns across multiple publications reveal gaps worth deeper study.

Integration is where the feedback cycle compounds. Over time, publications improve because each one builds on learning from previous ones.

**Example:** 
- Readers identified measurement gap → Project Atlas investigation: "How to objectively measure nervous system permission?"
- Readers identified time constraints → Next publication: "Permission in high-volume settings"
- Multiple publications mentioned clinician regulation → Thursday's article links to Monday's safety article, strengthening the alliance framework

## The Cycle

```
Curiosity (What's the question?)
    ↓
Research (What's the evidence?)
    ↓
Synthesis (What do we conclude?)
    ↓
Publication (How do we say it?)
    ↓
Distribution (Who needs to hear this?)
    ↓
Observation (How did it land?)
    ↓
Reflection (What did we learn?)
    ↓
Integration (How does this improve us?)
    ↓
    └─→ [Return to Curiosity with new questions]
```

## Success Criteria for the Knowledge Lifecycle

Every publication should be evaluated against institutional mission, not just social media metrics.

### Clarity Metrics
- **Hypothesis communicated?** Did readers understand what we were arguing for?
- **Logic coherent?** Did the evidence support the claim?
- **Examples helpful?** Did practical examples illuminate the concept?

### Impact Metrics
- **Meaningful discussion generated?** Comments should reflect genuine engagement with ideas, not just emotional reactions
- **Questions raised?** Did readers ask questions that suggest they're thinking beyond what we wrote?
- **Perspectives contributed?** Did readers add new angles or contradictions?

### Institutional Metrics
- **Website authority strengthened?** Does the article improve SEO for relevant queries? Does it link to other institutional work?
- **Educational material created?** Can this article be repurposed for Learn2Tape curriculum? CE content? Speaking materials?
- **Research questions identified?** Did audience feedback reveal gaps worth Project Atlas investigation?

### Brand Metrics
- **Tao brand reinforced?** Does the article communicate clinical philosophy, not techniques? Does it reflect the brand positioning?
- **Audience segment aligned?** Is engagement coming from target audience, or is the article reaching the wrong people?
- **Consistency maintained?** Does this article strengthen the institutional voice, or does it sound like a departure?

### Institutional Knowledge Metrics
- **Long-term value retained?** Will this article still be relevant and useful in two years?
- **Connections made?** Does this article link to our existing knowledge base, or does it stand alone?
- **Feedback loop closed?** Did we capture what we learned and feed it back into the institution?

## Knowledge vs. Content

This distinction matters.

**Content** is produced to be consumed. It's often disposable. Its value is in immediate engagement: likes, shares, comments. Once the moment passes, it's gone.

**Knowledge** is produced to endure. It's designed to be referenced, built upon, taught from. Its value increases over time as it connects to other knowledge and answers questions new audiences discover.

Finding My Wei publishes knowledge, not content. Every article should be something we'd be proud to stand behind in five years, or revise with explicit acknowledgment of what changed in our thinking.

---

## Governance

The Knowledge Lifecycle is **constitutional**. It does not change without institutional deliberation.

Individual stages may be refined (how we execute Research, how we measure Observation). But the eight stages and their purposes remain stable.

This stability allows:
- New team members to understand institutional priorities
- Standards to be applied consistently
- Long-term trust in institutional work
- Predictable feedback loops

---

## Why This Matters

Over time, this lifecycle creates a research institution.

**Without the lifecycle:** Publishing becomes broadcast. We publish articles, readers react, we move to the next article. No loop. No learning.

**With the lifecycle:** Publishing becomes research. Each publication informs the next investigation. Readers contribute expertise. Over 52 weeks, the institution becomes smarter.

The difference compounds. After one year of articles following this lifecycle:
- 52 permanent knowledge artifacts (blog articles)
- 52 measurement reports (what worked, what didn't)
- 52 learning reports (what we discovered)
- A prioritized research pipeline (Project Atlas investigations, identified by readers)
- An evolved institutional voice (refined by practice)

**That's institutional knowledge production.**
