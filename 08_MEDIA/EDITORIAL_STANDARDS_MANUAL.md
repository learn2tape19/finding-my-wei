## PART A: ACCURACY STANDARDS

### A1. Evidence Standards

**Standard:** Every factual claim must have evidence.

**What counts as evidence:**

✅ **Acceptable:**
- Peer-reviewed scientific research (published within 10 years preferred)
- Case studies from clinical practice
- Data from recognized organizations (WHO, NIH, APTA, NCBTMB)
- Textbooks by recognized authorities
- Professional guidelines and standards
- Direct clinical experience (labeled as such)

❌ **Not acceptable alone:**
- Anecdotal stories without pattern
- Unpublished claims by authorities
- Industry marketing materials
- Opinion stated as fact
- Untested hypotheses presented as knowledge

**Standard 1a: Primary Source Preference**

When possible, cite original research, not summaries.

- ❌ Bad: "Studies show massage improves circulation"
- ✅ Good: "In a 2019 study of 42 participants, Wilke et al. found increased blood flow velocity during massage, measured via Doppler ultrasound"

**Standard 1b: Evidence Hierarchy**

Present evidence in context of its strength:

- **Established fact** — "Mechanoreceptors respond to pressure" (decades of research, replicated)
- **Strong evidence** — "Vagal stimulation may reduce sympathetic activation" (multiple studies, consistent findings)
- **Emerging evidence** — "Some research suggests fascial hydration changes with manual therapy" (limited studies, promising but preliminary)
- **Hypothesis** — "We hypothesize that manual therapy may improve proprioceptive integration" (interesting idea, not yet tested)
- **Clinical observation** — "In my practice, I observe that calming the nervous system first improves outcomes" (my experience, not universal)

**Bad example:** "Manual therapy heals fascial damage and restructures tissue" (claims fact, no evidence, incorrect mechanism)

**Good example:** "While manual therapy doesn't restructure tissue, some research suggests it may improve tissue hydration and proprioceptive signaling through mechanoreceptor activation"

### A2. Citation Standards

**Standard:** Every source is credited and findable.

**For academic sources:**
- Author(s), Year, Title, Journal/Book, Volume, Pages
- Sufficient information for reader to find it

**For websites:**
- Full URL (not shortened links)
- Author name if available
- Publication date or access date
- Organization if individual author not identified

**For clinical observations:**
- Labeled as "clinical observation" or "in my experience"
- Never presented as universal truth
- Acknowledge it's specific to author's context

**For primary sources:**
- "According to Drew Freedman's unpublished research" (never pretend it's published)
- Distinguish between published and unpublished

### A3. Accuracy Verification

**Standard:** Before publication, every factual claim has been verified.

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Every statistic is checked against original source
- [ ] Names and dates are correct
- [ ] Technical terms are used correctly
- [ ] No exaggeration or misrepresentation of findings
- [ ] Competing evidence is acknowledged (not hidden)

**Red flags requiring double-check:**
- Claims that sound too good to be true
- Numbers presented without source
- Absolute statements ("always," "never," "proves")
- Statements that contradict what you said elsewhere

### A4. Uncertainty Standards

**Standard:** Acknowledge what we don't know.

**Required language:**

When evidence is mixed:
- "Research is mixed on this question..."
- "Some studies suggest... while others find..."
- "The evidence is inconclusive, but..."

When evidence is limited:
- "Limited research has examined..."
- "This remains an open question..."
- "We need more research to understand..."

When making inference:
- "While we can't directly measure X, we can infer from Y..."
- "This is a hypothesis based on..."
- "The evidence suggests this might be happening..."

When speaking from clinical experience:
- "In my clinical experience..."
- "I've observed that... though this may reflect my specific practice context"
- "My experience differs from published research in this way..."

**Never use:**
- "Proven to" when research is limited
- "Definitely causes" when mechanism is uncertain
- "Everyone agrees" when disagreement exists
- "Obviously" when it's actually complex

## PART B: CLARITY STANDARDS

### B1. Technical Language Standards

**Standard:** Technical terms are either defined or avoided.

**Option 1: Define the term**
- First use: "Proprioception (awareness of body position in space) guides movement"
- Subsequent use: "proprioceptive feedback"

**Option 2: Use simpler language**
- ❌ "Fascial hydration affects viscoelasticity"
- ✅ "How fascia holds water affects its flexibility"

**Option 3: Explain why the term matters**
- ❌ "Afferent feedback increases"
- ✅ "Afferent feedback (sensory input coming IN to the nervous system) increases"

**Never assume audience knows:**
- Anatomy beyond basic structure
- Technical terms from any discipline
- Abbreviations (spell out first use: "APTA (American Physical Therapy Association)")
- Professional jargon specific to one field

### B2. Structure Standards

**Standard:** Ideas are presented in logical sequence.

**For articles:**
1. **Opening** — Why should reader care? What's the question?
2. **Context** — What do we already know about this?
3. **Core idea** — Here's the new thinking
4. **Application** — How does this change practice?
5. **Conclusion** — What's the takeaway?

**For social posts:**
1. **Hook** — Capture attention in first line
2. **Idea** — Core insight or principle
3. **Why it matters** — Practical or conceptual value
4. **Call to action** — What's next? (Read more, think about this, share)

**For frameworks or lists:**
- **Introduction** — Why this framework?
- **Each section** — Concept → Application → Example
- **Integration** — How do sections connect?
- **Reflection** — How does reader use this?

### B3. Length Standards

**Standard:** Length is proportional to complexity and value.

**Guideline lengths:**

| Format | Ideal Length | Maximum |
|---|---|---|
| Social media post | 150-300 words | 500 |
| LinkedIn article | 800-1200 words | 2000 |
| Website article | 1500-3000 words | 5000 |
| Research paper | 5000-8000 words | 15000 |
| Newsletter section | 200-400 words | 600 |
| Quote/snippet | 1-2 sentences | 3 sentences |

**Standard 3a: Edit ruthlessly**

- Remove every word that doesn't add value
- Short paragraphs (2-4 sentences) for readability
- White space matters — dense text loses readers
- "If it can be cut, it should be cut"

### B4. Readability Standards

**Standard:** Text is accessible to intelligent professionals without specialized knowledge.

**Readability checklist:**
- [ ] No paragraph exceeds 5 sentences
- [ ] Sentences average under 20 words
- [ ] Complex sentences are followed by simpler ones
- [ ] Transitions connect ideas ("This is important because..." "However..." "As a result...")
- [ ] Key points are highlighted or repeated
- [ ] Metaphors clarify rather than obscure
- [ ] Examples are specific and relatable

**Red flags:**
- Paragraphs that exceed 7 sentences
- Sentences longer than 30 words in main text
- Three technical terms in one sentence
- Passive voice obscuring the actor

## PART C: RESPECT STANDARDS

### C1. Audience Respect Standards

**Standard:** Content respects audience expertise and intelligence.

**Don't:**
- Oversimplify complex topics to the point of inaccuracy
- Assume no prior knowledge when the audience has it
- Explain basic concepts to experts (wastes their time)
- Use condescending tone
- Assume one level of understanding across diverse audiences

**Do:**
- Acknowledge what audience already knows
- Start with their actual question or need
- Respect multiple perspectives
- Welcome intelligent disagreement
- Invite advanced readers to engage deeper

**Example — Bad (disrespectful):**
"You may not know this, but massage therapists have feelings too. Let me explain why you should be nice to your therapist..."

**Example — Good (respectful):**
"In professional relationships, the quality of the therapeutic alliance is mutual. Here's how clinicians can strengthen that two-way relationship..."

### C2. Colleague Respect Standards

**Standard:** Colleagues are treated fairly and given credit.

**When referencing competitors or disagreement:**
- [ ] Represent their position accurately (as they would describe it)
- [ ] Acknowledge the legitimate value in their approach
- [ ] Disagree on specific points, not people
- [ ] Credit original sources, not secondary summaries

**When sharing others' work:**
- [ ] Get permission when needed
- [ ] Link to original
- [ ] Give full credit
- [ ] Don't claim insights that aren't yours

**When criticizing approaches:**
- [ ] Critique the method, not the person
- [ ] Acknowledge context (their approach may be right for different situations)
- [ ] Offer constructive alternative
- [ ] Assume good intent

**Never:**
- Ad hominem attacks ("Dr. X is wrong because he's arrogant")
- Misrepresenting competitors' views
- Taking credit for others' work
- Dismissing colleagues as uninformed

### C3. Confidentiality Standards

**Standard:** Patient and clinical information is protected.

**Before publishing any clinical case:**
- [ ] Identifying information is changed (age, location, specific clinical details)
- [ ] Patient recognizes only their own case (others can't identify them)
- [ ] Learning point doesn't depend on identifying info
- [ ] No real names unless patient has given written permission

**For colleague information:**
- [ ] Get permission before quoting unpublished work
- [ ] Credit correctly
- [ ] Verify they're comfortable with attribution
- [ ] Don't share personal information without permission

**For organizational information:**
- [ ] Public information only, unless authorized
- [ ] Don't share internal business details
- [ ] Credit organizations when they support your work

## PART D: INTEGRITY STANDARDS

### D1. Conflict of Interest Standards

**Standard:** Financial and personal interests are disclosed.

**Disclose when:**
- You sell a product related to content
- You're employed by organization you're discussing
- You have financial interest in content success
- You have personal relationship with subject
- You're promoting your own courses or services

**How to disclose:**
- At beginning or end of article
- Clear and specific
- Explain why you're writing despite (or because of) the interest
- Let readers decide if it affects credibility

**Example disclosure:**
"*Disclosure: I developed the framework discussed in this article. I don't sell products based on it, but I do teach it through [program name]. I'm disclosing this because some readers may want to know I have professional investment in this approach being adopted.*"

### D2. Attribution Standards

**Standard:** Ideas, research, and language are credited to original sources.

**Plagiarism includes:**
- Using someone else's words without quotation marks
- Using their structure without acknowledgment
- Presenting their research as background knowledge
- Paraphrasing their ideas without credit

**How to attribute:**
- Direct quote: "Author Name says, '[exact quote]'" (Source)
- Paraphrase: "Following Author's thinking, X leads to Y" (Author, Year)
- Framework: "Building on Author's framework of X..."

**What doesn't need attribution:**
- General knowledge ("massage increases blood flow")
- Your own clinical experience
- Widely known facts (date of publication, author names)

### D3. Correction Standards

**Standard:** Errors are corrected promptly and publicly.

**When you find an error:**
1. Correct the original (update the post, article, or document)
2. Add a note explaining the correction
3. If significant, notify people who read the original
4. Learn to prevent recurrence

**Format for correction:**
- *Correction (added June 28): The original said "X," but research shows "Y." Thank you to [Name] for the catch.*

**Don't:**
- Hide errors by quietly fixing them
- Pretend you meant to say the correct thing
- Delete evidence of the error

## PART E: VOICE & TONE STANDARDS

### E1. Professional Voice Standards

**Standard:** Voice is professional but human.

**Professional means:**
- Knowledgeable and confident in subject matter
- Respectful to audience and colleagues
- Evidence-based reasoning
- Clear thinking about complex topics
- Appropriate formality for context

**Human means:**
- Direct first-person when appropriate ("In my experience...")
- Conversational language (not overly formal)
- Honesty about uncertainty
- Personal perspective (not pretending to be objective)
- Personality and perspective evident in writing

**Not this:**
- "The clinician may observe..." (passive, overly formal)
- "It is known that..." (pretending to be objective)
- "One might suggest..." (hiding perspective)

**Try this:**
- "In my observation..."
- "Research shows..."
- "I think this matters because..."

### E2. Confidence Calibration Standards

**Standard:** Confidence level matches evidence level.

**High confidence language** (strong evidence, multiple studies, replicated findings):
- "Manual therapy activates mechanoreceptors"
- "This approach is clinically effective"
- "The evidence clearly supports..."

**Moderate confidence language** (good evidence, but limited studies or context-dependent):
- "Manual therapy appears to stimulate proprioceptive feedback"
- "This approach is effective for many patients"
- "Research suggests this mechanism..."

**Low confidence language** (preliminary evidence, emerging research, hypothesis):
- "Manual therapy may influence nervous system regulation"
- "This approach shows promise in early research"
- "We hypothesize that..."
- "Some evidence points toward..."

**Never:**
- Use high confidence language for weak evidence
- Hide certainty level from readers
- Pretend hypothesis is established fact

### E3. Tone for Different Contexts

| Context | Tone | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Research paper** | Scholarly, precise, measured | "A 2023 meta-analysis identified limited evidence for..." |
| **Educational article** | Clear, accessible, encouraging | "Here's how to think about the nervous system in your work..." |
| **Case reflection** | Personal, humble, curious | "This case challenged my thinking because..." |
| **Professional commentary** | Direct, thoughtful, respectful challenge | "While this approach has merit, I'd offer this perspective..." |
| **Social media** | Engaging, concrete, memorable | "Your nervous system is literally listening to your movement." |
| **Newsletter** | Conversational, curated, connective | "This week I'm thinking about how X connects to Y..." |

## PART F: CONTENT-SPECIFIC STANDARDS

### F1. Research Translation Standards

**When translating research into practical content:**

**Do:**
- [ ] Explain what researchers actually studied (not what you wish they studied)
- [ ] Acknowledge study limitations (sample size, population, methodology)
- [ ] Separate findings from interpretation
- [ ] Explain why this matters to practitioners
- [ ] Suggest how to apply (or not apply) findings

**Don't:**
- Overgeneralize from limited studies
- Suggest implications beyond what data supports
- Hide methodological weaknesses
- Present association as causation
- Claim "proof" for correlational data

**Example — Bad:**
"This study proves massage therapy heals tissue damage and promotes healing."

**Example — Good:**
"This study of 30 patients found that soft tissue mobilization temporarily increased blood flow to the area. However, it didn't directly measure tissue healing, only blood flow changes. This supports the theory that manual therapy may create conditions for healing, but we can't conclude it 'heals tissue' from this study alone."

### F2. Case Reflection Standards

**When writing from clinical experience:**

**Do:**
- [ ] Describe specific context (population, setting, your training level)
- [ ] Acknowledge this reflects your approach, not universal truth
- [ ] Explain your reasoning transparently
- [ ] Note where this contradicts or aligns with evidence
- [ ] Invite alternative interpretations

**Don't:**
- Generalize from one case to all patients
- Present one approach as "the way"
- Hide failed cases (learning includes what didn't work)
- Claim to have discovered something everyone else missed

**Example — Bad:**
"I discovered that nervous system state is more important than technique. All therapists should focus on this."

**Example — Good:**
"In my practice, I've noticed that when I prioritize creating safety before applying technique, patients consistently report better outcomes. This aligns with research on the therapeutic alliance, though my observation is specific to my practice context and clinical population."

### F3. Opinion & Perspective Standards

**When offering perspective:**

**Do:**
- [ ] Label it clearly ("I think..." "In my view..." "This is my take...")
- [ ] Explain your reasoning
- [ ] Acknowledge opposing viewpoints
- [ ] Avoid false objectivity ("research shows" vs. "I interpret research to mean")

**Don't:**
- Sneak perspective in as fact
- Pretend you're objective when you're advocating
- Dismiss disagreement without engaging it
- Attack opponents instead of ideas

**Example — Bad:**
"The right way to work is to focus on the nervous system first."

**Example — Good:**
"I've shifted my approach toward nervous system regulation first because I think it makes the most sense given what we know about how change happens. I respect colleagues who prioritize technique first, but I've found that safety usually needs to come first for my patients to make lasting changes."

### F4. Promotional Standards

**When promoting products, courses, or services:**

**Promotion should be:**
- [ ] 3% or less of all content
- [ ] Labeled clearly as promotion
- [ ] Honest about benefits and limitations
- [ ] Positioned as "if this might help, here it is" not "you must do this"
- [ ] Value-first (earning trust before asking for purchase)

**Don't:**
- Use testimonials without context ("Changed my practice" could mean many things)
- Make unsubstantiated claims about results
- Create artificial urgency ("Only 3 spots left!")
- Hide promotional intent behind editorial content

**Example — Bad:**
"You need this certification to be a real therapist."

**Example — Good:**
"We offer a certification in K-Cuts taping that covers research, application, and business integration. Some therapists find this valuable for building their practice. If you're interested, here's what's included."

## PART G: PLATFORM-SPECIFIC STANDARDS

### G1. LinkedIn Standards

**Standard:** Professional, idea-focused, conversation-starting

**LinkedIn post should:**
- [ ] Open with hook (first line answers "why should I read?")
- [ ] Address professional growth or thinking
- [ ] Encourage comments/discussion
- [ ] Avoid over-promotion
- [ ] Use line breaks for readability
- [ ] Include relevant hashtags (3-5)

**Length:** 800-1200 words optimal

**Tone:** Professional but personable, thought-leader perspective

**Example opening:**
"I've been thinking about why some therapists create better outcomes than others. It's not always about technique."

### G2. Instagram Standards

**Standard:** Visual, memorable, shareable

**Instagram post should:**
- [ ] Strong visual (image or carousel)
- [ ] Caption under 150 words (or visible before "...more")
- [ ] One clear idea
- [ ] Shareable insight or practical tip
- [ ] Call to action (save, share, or visit link)
- [ ] Hashtags separate (avoid in caption)

**Carousel standards (5-7 slides):**
- [ ] Each slide one idea
- [ ] Visual reinforces text
- [ ] Progressive complexity (simple → detailed)
- [ ] Slide 1: Hook, what they'll learn
- [ ] Last slide: Key takeaway + call to action

**Tone:** Accessible, encouraging, practical

**Example:**
Slide 1: "Your nervous system doesn't distinguish between threat and opportunity."
Slide 2: "It only knows: Safe or Not Safe"
Slide 3: "And it makes these decisions in milliseconds"
Slide 4: "Before your conscious mind catches up"
Slide 5: "That's why your vibe matters before your technique does"

### G3. Email Newsletter Standards

**Standard:** Curated, connective, valuable

**Newsletter should:**
- [ ] Personal voice (from Drew)
- [ ] 3-4 sections curated around a theme
- [ ] Mix of educational, commentary, and life/practice
- [ ] Clear but not pushy CTAs
- [ ] 1000-1500 words total
- [ ] Scannable structure (headings, short paragraphs)

**Sections:**
1. **What I'm thinking** — Opening reflection
2. **What I learned** — Curated article/research highlight
3. **What to do** — Practical application
4. **What to read** — Resource or related content

### G4. Website Article Standards

**Standard:** Comprehensive, searchable, evergreen

**Article should:**
- [ ] Detailed enough to be referenced (2000-3000 words)
- [ ] SEO-optimized (keyword in title and first paragraph)
- [ ] Scannable with headings and subheadings
- [ ] Internal links to related content
- [ ] External links to sources
- [ ] Updated annually or when new evidence emerges
- [ ] Author bio at bottom

**Structure:**
1. **Title** (clear, keyword-rich)
2. **Intro** (what question does this answer?)
3. **Context** (what do we already know?)
4. **Analysis** (here's the thinking)
5. **Application** (how to use this)
6. **Conclusion** (key takeaways)
7. **Resources** (further reading)

## PART H: FACT-CHECKING STANDARDS

### H1. Before Publication Checklist

**Evidence:**
- [ ] Every claim has evidence or is labeled as observation/hypothesis
- [ ] Sources are cited correctly and completely
- [ ] Study limitations are acknowledged
- [ ] No exaggeration of findings
- [ ] Uncertainty is expressed appropriately

**Accuracy:**
- [ ] Facts checked against authoritative sources
- [ ] Names spelled correctly
- [ ] Dates verified
- [ ] Statistics confirmed
- [ ] Technical terms used correctly
- [ ] No false claims

**Clarity:**
- [ ] Technical terms defined or simplified
- [ ] Structure follows logical sequence
- [ ] Length is appropriate
- [ ] Readability is high
- [ ] Key points stand out

**Respect:**
- [ ] Colleagues treated fairly
- [ ] Opposing views acknowledged
- [ ] Tone is professional
- [ ] Confidentiality protected
- [ ] No attacks on people

**Integrity:**
- [ ] Conflicts of interest disclosed
- [ ] Attribution complete
- [ ] No plagiarism
- [ ] Opinion labeled as opinion
- [ ] Promotion labeled as promotion

**Alignment:**
- [ ] Matches editorial voice
- [ ] Fits audience
- [ ] Serves stated purpose
- [ ] Proud to publish in 10 years

## PART I: DISPUTE RESOLUTION

### When Editorial Standards Are Unclear

**Process:**
1. **Identify the conflict** — Which standard is in question?
2. **Check Constitution** — What value is at stake?
3. **Review similar precedents** — How was this handled before?
4. **Consult with Editor-in-Chief** — What's the principle?
5. **Document decision** — Why this choice?

**Common disputes:**

| Question | Standard | Resolution |
|---|---|---|
| How much evidence is enough? | Accuracy A1 | Evidence strength must match confidence language |
| Can I simplify this complex topic? | Clarity B1 | Simplification is ok if accuracy isn't lost |
| Can I promote my course? | Promotion F4 | Yes, if it's 3% of content and clearly labeled |
| Should I mention disagreement? | Respect C2 | Always acknowledge legitimate alternatives |
| Can I use this client story? | Confidentiality C3 | Only if truly unidentifiable without written permission |
