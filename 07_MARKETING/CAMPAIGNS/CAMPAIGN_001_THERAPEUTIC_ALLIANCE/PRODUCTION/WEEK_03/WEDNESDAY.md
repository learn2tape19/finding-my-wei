## Editorial Purpose

Wednesday moves the campaign from Tuesday's transition into a practical clinical behavior: observe ordinary movement before coaching, testing, correcting, or interpreting it.

**Primary teaching point:** Rising from a chair, removing a jacket, shifting weight, initiating gait, changing position, and breathing during movement may offer useful information before formal testing begins. Premature coaching changes the behavior being observed.

**Primary line:** Let the body show you how it organizes before you try to reorganize it.

**Engagement question:** What everyday movement tells you the most before formal assessment begins?

**CTA:** Save the post. In your next appropriate session, pause before you cue and notice what changes when you wait.

### Final LinkedIn copy

```text
Assessment begins before formal testing.

In clinical education, we devote considerable attention to which tests to perform and how to interpret the findings. But useful information may appear before the testing sequence begins.

How does the patient rise from the chair? Remove a jacket? Shift weight? Initiate gait? Change position? Breathe during movement?

These moments may offer context about ease, hesitation, rhythm, breath, and confidence.

There is also a methodological problem worth noticing: the moment we cue, correct, or demonstrate, we change the behavior we are attempting to observe.

Coaching still has a place. The question is whether we have observed clearly before we begin to influence the movement.

Let the body show you how it organizes before you try to reorganize it.

What everyday movement gives you the most useful information before formal assessment begins?
```
