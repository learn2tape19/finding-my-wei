# The Greet Is the First Clinical Intervention

Your patient walks in. They've just stepped out of their day. The tension. The pace. The momentum of whatever they were doing before they got to your door.

They're not yet in a space where their body can be assessed clearly. They're not yet regulated enough for useful history-taking. Their nervous system is still answering the questions of the world they just left.

And for most clinicians, this is exactly when we begin our session.

We greet them quickly. We move them toward the table. We start our evaluation. We get to work.

What if this is backward?

What if the clinical work has already begun? Not when we place our hands. When they arrive.

---

## The Greet Is Clinical

The traditional idea treats greeting as courtesy before work. A warming up. Small talk before the real business.

But that framework misses something fundamental. The patient's nervous system is constantly working to answer one question: Is this safe?

They don't ask it consciously. Their nervous system is asking it. Through the pace of the room. Through your posture. Through whether you make eye contact. Through whether you seem present or distracted. Through the predictability of what happens next.

A thorough greet isn't wasting time before your clinical work. It's doing essential clinical work before you ever touch them.

The greet is where the patient's nervous system gets its first reliable answer about whether this moment is different from the momentum they arrived with.

---

## You Are Part of the Environment

The clinician is not a neutral force applying a technique to an isolated tissue problem. You are part of the clinical environment your patient's nervous system is reading.

Your pace affects their pace. Your calmness or hurriedness gets interpreted before you speak. Your orientation toward them registers. Whether you seem genuinely focused on this specific person or managing your schedule, they read it.

This isn't new age thinking. This is observable neurobiology. The nervous system of one person influences the nervous system of another through what's called co-regulation. When you regulate your own system, the person in front of you has an easier time organizing theirs.

The greet is where this co-regulation either begins or fails to begin.

If you're still moving at the pace of your last appointment when your next patient enters, they inherit that pace. If you arrive regulated and present, that regulated presence is available to them.

---

## Observation Before Performance

Most clinicians have been trained to gather information quickly. Get the history. Document the limitations. Move to assessment.

But there's a gap between what a guarded nervous system reports and what it's actually capable of.

When a patient comes in tense, uncertain, and vigilant, they will show you protection. They will guard against pain. They will limit motion. They will report limitation.

This is not accurate data about their capacity. It's accurate data about their current state.

A greet that genuinely helps them transition reduces that guarding. It creates space for more honest information to emerge.

This is why the first minutes matter more than many clinicians realize. Not because you're building rapport, though that happens. But because you're actually shifting the system's ability to report what's true.

---

## Orientation Reduces Uncertainty

The human nervous system is efficient. When it encounters something unknown, it prepares for threat.

Silence about what's coming next. Unexplained transitions. Techniques that arrive without warning. A clinician who seems to know where this is going but isn't saying.

All of these create low-level vigilance.

Contrast this with what happens when a patient knows what's next. When you explain what you're about to do. When you move predictably. When the session has a clear shape.

The nervous system can settle. It doesn't need to anticipate surprise.

This is why a good greet includes clarity about what this session will include. It's not just kindness. It's neurology.

Tell them how long you'll take. Tell them what will happen first. Tell them how they should position themselves. Remove the question marks. Replace them with a clear, predictable shape to the work.

---

## Listening Without Steering

There's a difference between listening that gathers data quickly and listening that creates safety.

One kind of listening is efficient. You ask specific questions. You steer toward the information that matters for your assessment. You listen for what confirms your hypothesis.

The other kind of listening is broader. You listen without steering them toward your conclusion. You notice what they bring to the conversation without pushing them toward the medical model.

Not all listening is the same. The kind that creates safety is the kind where the patient feels genuinely heard rather than interrogated.

During the greet, this distinction matters. If they feel questioned, they tense. If they feel heard, they settle.

---

## Predictability Precedes Reliable Assessment

Here's the clinical outcome: when the nervous system is more regulated, the assessment is more accurate.

A patient who has arrived still in the momentum of their day will guard tissues you could otherwise access. They'll report pain you could otherwise modify. They'll show you compensation you could otherwise resolve.

None of this is their fault. It's how nervous systems work.

A greet that creates transition. One that offers regulation through presence, predictability, and genuine orientation. This changes what the nervous system reports.

Then your assessment of mobility, tissue quality, and limitation is more useful. Your treatment plan is based on actual capacity, not protective guarding.

This is why the best clinicians seem to get better results. They're not necessarily more skilled technically. They're creating conditions for accurate information before they attempt to change anything.

---

## The Self-Audit

Before your next patient, ask yourself:

When they arrived, what pace were you moving at?

Were you fully oriented to them, or was part of your attention on the schedule?

Did you explain what the session would include, or did you move into evaluation?

Did you listen to them, or listen for specific information?

How much time passed before you moved to the table?

None of these answers are right or wrong. They're just information about what your patient actually experienced.

If you're moving quickly and oriented to your schedule, your patient experiences that. If you're calm and present, they experience that too.

The greet is offering your nervous system state to theirs as an organizing influence.

---

## Closing

The clinical session begins before the formal assessment. The session begins when the patient arrives. What happens in those first moments determines what will be possible during everything that follows. Your pace. Your clarity. Your presence. These elements shape the foundation.

This isn't sentimentality. It's physiology.

The greet is not the time before clinical work. The greet is where the clinical work begins.
