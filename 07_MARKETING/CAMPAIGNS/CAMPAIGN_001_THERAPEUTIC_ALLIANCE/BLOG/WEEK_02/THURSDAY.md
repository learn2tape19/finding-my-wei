# The Greet Is the First Clinical Intervention

Your patient walks in. They're still carrying the momentum of their day—the tension, the pace, whatever happened before they arrived at your door.

These first moments are often treated as a transition into the session. We greet the patient, bring them into the treatment space, and begin gathering information.

But consider a different possibility: the clinical work has already started. Not when you touch them. When they arrive.

---

## The Greet as Clinical Act

The traditional view treats greeting as courtesy before work—small talk that precedes the real business. A warm-up. A social transition.

But something more may happen during those first moments. The patient's nervous system is actively gathering information. About your pace. Your presence. Whether you seem focused on them or distracted. Whether this session has a shape to it or unfolds unpredictably.

The greet isn't wasting time before clinical work. It can function as clinical work itself—an opportunity to observe how someone arrives, to orient them to what's coming, and to establish whether this moment feels manageable and predictable to them.

Consider: a patient who feels oriented, heard, and clear about what comes next may report different information during assessment than one who remains uncertain about the process. Not because one patient is truthful and another isn't. Because how someone shows up clinically may be influenced by how certain they feel about what's happening to them.

---

## You Are Part of the Clinical Picture

The clinician is not separate from the clinical environment. Your state, your presence, your pace—these may influence what the patient's nervous system encounters when they enter your space.

Your pace can influence their pacing. Your presence registers before you speak. Whether you seem fully engaged with this specific person, or partially oriented toward your schedule, or moving quickly between tasks—these cues are available to them.

This touches on what neuroscience calls co-regulation: the nervous system of one person may influence the state of another's nervous system. It doesn't mean one person's state determines another's outcome. But it may contribute to conditions where regulation becomes more accessible or where vigilance persists.

If you arrive regulated and present, that state exists in the room. If you're still moving at the pace of your last appointment, or holding the stress of your morning, that lives in the space too. The patient doesn't need to understand cognitively what's happening. Their response may be influenced by it, even if they never consciously identify the source.

---

## What a Guarded System Reports

Most clinicians have been trained to gather information quickly: get the history, document the limitations, move to assessment.

Guarding provides meaningful information about how the patient is responding in that moment. It may also obscure aspects of baseline movement or capacity that become clearer as uncertainty decreases.

When a patient arrives tense and uncertain, they may guard tissues, report heightened limitations, restrict motion. This is clinically useful information. It tells you their current protective state. It tells you what they're expressing through protection. But it may not represent the full picture of what's accessible to them when they feel more certain about safety.

A greet that helps someone transition from uncertainty toward clarity may create conditions where different information becomes available during assessment. This doesn't mean the patient was being dishonest before. It means that what someone can show you often depends, in part, on how safe they feel. A protected system shows you protection. A less guarded system may show you something different.

---

## Predictability and Orientation

When a patient doesn't know what comes next, their attention may organize around anticipating threat. Silence about what you're going to do. Unexplained transitions between types of touch. A clinician who clearly knows what's happening but isn't sharing that information.

All of these may sustain uncertainty.

When a patient knows what to expect—how long this will take, what will happen first, how they should position themselves—some of that uncertainty may ease. They have a frame. They can prepare. They're not in constant anticipation.

This clarity may help the clinician distinguish between findings that persist and responses influenced by uncertainty about what will happen next.

---

## How You Listen Matters

There are different ways to listen. One way is purposeful and efficient: you ask specific questions designed to confirm or rule out hypotheses. You steer toward what matters for your working clinical picture. You listen for what supports your working assessment.

Another way is broader and less directed: you listen without steering them toward your conclusion. You notice what they bring without immediately pushing it toward the medical model or diagnostic category.

The listening that may support a patient feeling safe is the kind where they experience being genuinely heard rather than interrogated. When the interaction feels interrogative or overly directed, it may influence how the patient responds. Listening without prematurely steering the account can preserve information that might otherwise be missed.

During the greet, this distinction matters.

---

## The Self-Audit

Before your next patient, notice:

When they arrived, what pace were you moving at? Were you fully oriented to them, or was some of your attention still on your schedule or your previous appointment?

Did you explain what the session would include, or move quickly into evaluation? Did you listen to what they brought, or listen primarily for specific diagnostic information?

These aren't right-or-wrong questions. They're observations about what actually happened in that first encounter.

If you were moving quickly and thinking about your next patient, they experienced that. If you were calm and fully present, they experienced that too. What matters is noticing what was actually there, not judging it.

---

## Closing

The clinical session doesn't begin when you place your hands. It begins when the patient arrives. What happens in those first moments may create conditions for what becomes possible during everything that follows.

The greet is not the time before clinical work. It's a moment where clinical work can begin.
