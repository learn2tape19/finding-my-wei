## Research Question

What is the difference between informed consent and nervous system permission? How does a clinician deliberately earn permission before intervention?

---

## Core Argument (3–5 Key Points)

1. **Informed consent is cognitive; permission is physiological.** Legal agreement doesn't mean the nervous system has stopped defending. Permission is a biological state created through communication, presence, pacing, observation, and safety.

2. **The question precedes the touch.** Before hands make contact, the clinician must ask: "Have I earned permission from this nervous system?" That question reframes the entire clinical encounter.

3. **Permission is earned, not assumed.** It's built through eye contact, pacing, explanation, warning before touch, observation of the patient's state. It cannot be forced or negotiated through authority.

4. **Permission changes tissue accessibility.** Once permission exists, tissue reorganizes more readily, movement returns more easily, pain decreases more reliably. This is measurable and clinical, not metaphorical.

5. **Permission is learned.** Clinicians can develop permission-earning as a skill—more reliably than mastering any technique.
