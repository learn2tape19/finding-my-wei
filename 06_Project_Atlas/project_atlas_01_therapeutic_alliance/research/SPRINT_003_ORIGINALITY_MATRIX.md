- Placebo neurobiology (Benedetti, Kaptchuk) — context and nervous system effects

**None**. All major mechanisms are already documented.

None. We apply existing mechanisms to manual therapy context, but we don't discover new ones.

### Degree of Originality
**Zero**. We are explicating known science, not discovering new science.

### Strength of Evidence
N/A — not an original claim.

### Publication Potential
**No**. Cannot publish "here are existing mechanisms applied to manual therapy" as original scientific discovery.

## B. Original Theoretical Integration

**Question**: Does Atlas integrate multiple established fields into a coherent explanatory framework?

### Existing Literature
- **Interpersonal neurobiology** (Siegel, 2012) — integrates neuroscience + development + relationships
- **Sensorimotor psychotherapy** (Ogden et al., 2006) — integrates body + emotion + cognition
- **Integrative medicine frameworks** (Reiger et al., 2016) — attempt multiple-field integration
- **Contextual model of psychotherapy** (Wampold, 2015) — integrates expectations + relationship + culture + technique
- **Somatic experiencing** (Levine, 1997+) — integrates trauma + nervous system + body

### Current Gap
Most integration frameworks are either:
- Too broad (trying to explain all healing, losing specificity)
- Focused on psychotherapy (not hands-on modalities)
- Focused on trauma (not routine clinical work)
- **Missing**: Specific framework for *manual therapy alliance* integrating nervous system + communication + context + sensorimotor + autonomy

### Atlas Contribution
Integrates five specific domains into manual therapy alliance model:
1. Clinician nervous system regulation (polyvagal)
2. Attuned communication (attachment + neuroscience)
3. Meaningful context (placebo/contextual effects)
4. Sensorimotor learning (neuroscience + somatic)
5. Preserved autonomy (permission + self-determination theory)

**Novelty level**: Medium. The integration itself is somewhat new, but:
- Siegel already does multi-domain integration
- Ogden already integrates body + mind + relationship
- The five domains we chose are reasonable but not obviously *the* definitive framework
- Other integrations might work equally well (e.g., Wampold's contextual model + attachment)

### Strength of Evidence
**Good**, but not unique. Evidence supports each domain separately; integration is logical but not empirically proven as superior to other possible integrations.

### Publication Potential
**Medium**. Could publish as "integrative framework for manual therapy alliance" but would need to:
- Acknowledge this is one possible integration, not the only one
- Provide evidence that THIS integration is better than alternatives
- Show clinical outcomes improve with this framework vs. others

**Risk**: Reviewers may say "we could integrate these differently and get the same explanatory power."

## C. Original Clinical Framework

**Question**: Does Atlas produce a practical clinical model not currently available?

### Existing Literature
- **Patient-centered care models** — focus on communication + respect
- **Shared decision-making frameworks** — involve patients in planning
- **Therapeutic presence models** — emphasize clinician attunement
- **Trauma-informed care** — nervous system-aware approach
- **Attachment-informed practice** — relationship-based clinical model
- **Pain science education** — context-aware patient communication

### Current Gap
**Yes, a specific gap exists**: No unified clinical framework specifically teaches manual therapists how to build alliance through:
- Conscious nervous system regulation (not just "be present")
- Specific communication techniques (not just "listen")
- Permission-centered approach (not just "inform consent")
- Touch-based safety (not just relational safety)

Current frameworks are either:
- For psychotherapy (50-week relationships, different timescale)
- For trauma (assumes pathology; routine manual therapy is not trauma treatment)
- Generic (not specific to manual therapy)

### Atlas Contribution
A **clinician-facing framework** that teaches:
1. How to regulate your own nervous system intentionally
2. How to communicate safety through specific words, tone, presence
3. How to create meaning and expectation appropriately
4. How to provide touch that updates nervous system predictions
5. How to preserve client autonomy through permission-centered approach

**Specificity**: Very specific to manual therapy context (not psychotherapy, not trauma work per se)

**Teachability**: Concrete and actionable (clinicians can learn and practice these behaviors)

### Strength of Evidence
**Moderate**. Evidence supports each element separately, but no evidence yet proves:
- Clinicians taught this framework perform better
- Clients of trained clinicians show better outcomes
- This framework is better than other possible clinical models

### Publication Potential
**Strong** — as a clinical framework paper (not research discovery). Could publish as:
- "Alliance-Based Clinical Framework for Manual Therapy"
- "Nervous System-Informed Approach to Therapeutic Presence"
- Practitioner-facing article in manual therapy journals

Would need outcome data comparing trained vs. untrained clinicians.

## E. Original Terminology

**Question**: Does "Permission" describe something genuinely new?

### Existing Literature
- **Autonomy support** (Self-Determination Theory) — respecting client choice
- **Shared decision-making** — involving patient in planning
- **Informed consent** — ensuring understanding and agreement
- **Patient-centered care** — treatment aligned with patient values
- **Therapeutic empowerment** — supporting client agency

### Current Gap
These concepts exist, but Permission adds something:
- Not just cognitive agreement (informed consent)
- Not just involvement in planning (shared decision-making)
- Not just value alignment (patient-centered care)
- **New element**: nervous system basis for safety-enough-to-say-no

Permission = autonomy support + nervous system safety state

### Atlas Contribution
Reframes autonomy support through nervous system lens:
- "Can you say no and still feel safe?" (nervous system + autonomy)
- Not just ethical requirement but foundational nervous system state
- Teachable skill (create conditions where "no" feels safe)
- Measurable outcome (client reports safety to decline)

### Degree of Originality
**Low-to-Medium**. Permission is primarily **better language** for existing concepts.

**Honest assessment**: It's not a new mechanism. It's a useful reframing. The innovation is:
- Emphasizing the *nervous system* component of autonomy
- Making it central to alliance (not peripheral)
- Creating a teaching framework around it

### Strength of Evidence
**Weak**. No empirical evidence yet that permission-centered approach produces better outcomes than autonomy-support-centered approach.

### Publication Potential
**Medium-to-Low** as standalone contribution. Could publish as:
- Part of larger clinical framework paper (not standalone)
- Conceptual paper reframing permission in nervous system terms
- Teaching methodology paper

Risk: Reviewers may say "this is just autonomy support with different language."

## F. Original Research Agenda

**Question**: Does Atlas identify a new direction for future investigation?

### Existing Literature
- Psychotherapy alliance research (85+ years, well-established)
- Nervous system co-regulation research (growing, but not applied to brief manual therapy)
- Manual therapy outcomes research (technique-focused, relationship-light)
- **Gap**: No systematic investigation of alliance mechanisms in brief-encounter manual therapy

### Current Gap
**Yes, substantial gap**:
- How does alliance develop in 1-hour encounters (vs. 50-week psychotherapy)?
- Do nervous system markers (HRV, vagal tone) predict alliance perception?
- Does explicit permission-focused approach improve outcomes vs. implicit autonomy?
- Can manual therapists learn to regulate nervous system intentionally? Does it improve outcomes?
- Is manual therapy alliance mechanistically distinct from psychotherapy alliance?

### Atlas Contribution
Defines specific, testable questions:
1. **Nervous system markers**: Do physiological measures (HRV, vagal tone, skin conductance) correlate with client-reported alliance?
2. **Permission effects**: Do clients with explicit permission opportunity report higher alliance and outcomes?
3. **Brief-encounter mechanisms**: How does alliance develop differently in 1-hour vs. 50-week relationships?
4. **Clinician training effects**: Do clinicians trained in nervous system regulation produce better alliance and outcomes?
5. **Modality comparison**: Is manual therapy alliance mechanistically different from psychotherapy alliance?

### Degree of Originality
**High**. These questions are genuinely not being systematically investigated.

Psychotherapy alliance research doesn't examine brief manual therapy.
Manual therapy research doesn't systematically examine alliance.
Nervous system research doesn't apply to therapeutic alliance measurement.

### Strength of Evidence
**N/A** — these are future research questions, not current evidence.

### Publication Potential
**Very strong**. Could publish as:
- "Research Agenda for Understanding Alliance in Manual Therapy"
- Series of research papers testing each question
- Foundation for 5-10 year research program
