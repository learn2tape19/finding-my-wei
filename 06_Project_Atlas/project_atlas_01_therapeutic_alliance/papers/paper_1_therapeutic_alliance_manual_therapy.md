# The Therapeutic Alliance in Manual Therapy: A Conceptual Review and Research Agenda

**Word Count**: ~5,400 words

## Abstract

The therapeutic alliance — the working relationship between clinician and client — is one of the most consistently replicated predictors of outcome in psychotherapy research, with a robust meta-analytic correlation of r = .21–.27 across theoretical orientations. Nearly all of this evidence base, however, comes from talk-based psychotherapy delivered across extended courses of weekly treatment. Manual therapy — physical therapy, massage therapy, chiropractic, osteopathy, and related hands-on disciplines — differs from this context in time scale (single sessions to a few weeks, rather than months), in primary medium (touch rather than verbal exchange), and in primary outcome target (functional and physical change rather than psychological insight). Whether, and how, alliance operates in this very different clinical context is not well established. This paper reviews what is currently known about therapeutic relationship factors in manual therapy, identifies the specific ways manual therapy's brief, embodied, function-oriented structure may change how alliance forms and operates, and proposes a research agenda to address seven concrete gaps in the literature. We do not claim that manual therapy alliance is mechanistically distinct from psychotherapy alliance — that claim is, itself, one of the open questions this paper raises.

---

## 1. Introduction

Decades of psychotherapy research converge on a single finding that has proven unusually durable across theoretical schools, client populations, and treatment formats: the quality of the working relationship between clinician and client — the *therapeutic alliance* — predicts treatment outcome. Meta-analyses spanning hundreds of studies put this relationship at r = .21–.27 (Norcross, 2011; Horvath et al., 2011), and more recent work confirms the effect remains robust when early alliance, rather than alliance averaged across treatment, is used as the predictor (Flückiger et al., 2018). Relationship factors as a category — of which alliance is the most studied — are estimated to account for as much as 30% of outcome variance in psychotherapy (Lambert & Barley, 2001).

This body of evidence was built almost entirely within one clinical format: weekly, 50-minute, talk-based sessions extended over months. That format is not the format in which manual therapists work. A physical therapist, massage therapist, chiropractor, or osteopathic clinician typically meets a client for a small number of sessions — often a single visit, frequently fewer than six — delivered primarily through physical contact rather than verbal exchange, oriented toward a functional or physical endpoint (reduced pain, restored movement, improved tissue mobility) rather than psychological insight.

This raises a basic and so far inadequately answered question: **does alliance, as understood in the psychotherapy literature, operate the same way in brief, hands-on, function-oriented clinical encounters — or does the manual therapy context change how alliance forms, what it does, and how it should be measured?**

### Why the Question Matters

Manual therapists collectively deliver an enormous volume of direct, hands-on clinical contact. If relationship factors contribute meaningfully to outcome in this setting — as they appear to in psychotherapy — then training programs, clinical guidelines, and outcome measurement in manual therapy are currently operating with little formal guidance on a variable that may matter as much as technique. Conversely, if alliance in manual therapy turns out to function quite differently than it does in psychotherapy — forming on a different timescale, through different channels, serving different outcome pathways — then importing psychotherapy alliance constructs and instruments wholesale risks measuring the wrong thing.

Neither possibility has been adequately tested. The purpose of this paper is not to resolve the question, but to state it precisely, review what existing literature can and cannot tell us about it, and propose a structured agenda for the research that would resolve it.

### Scope and Approach

This paper is a conceptual and narrative review, not a systematic review or meta-analysis. Its contribution is not new data but the explicit identification of a structural gap between two well-developed but largely non-overlapping literatures — psychotherapy alliance research and manual therapy outcomes research — and a research agenda built from that gap. We deliberately avoid claiming, at this stage, that manual therapy alliance is mechanistically unique. That is a hypothesis the field needs to test, not a conclusion this paper is positioned to draw.

## 2. What Psychotherapy Alliance Research Tells Us

### The Core Findings

The construct most commonly used to operationalize therapeutic relationship quality is Bordin's (1979) tripartite model: alliance as agreement on treatment *goals*, agreement on *tasks*, and the relational *bond* between clinician and client. This model has proven durable for more than four decades and underlies the most widely used alliance instrument, the Working Alliance Inventory (WAI; Horvath & Greenberg, 1989).

Across meta-analyses, alliance correlates with outcome at r = .21–.27, a relationship that holds across psychodynamic, cognitive-behavioral, humanistic, and integrative treatment approaches (Norcross, 2011; Horvath et al., 2011). Flückiger et al. (2018) confirm that this is not an artifact of retrospective measurement: alliance assessed early in treatment (by session 3–5) predicts later outcome, suggesting the relationship is not purely a byproduct of clients who are already improving reporting better rapport.

### How Alliance Is Measured

The WAI remains the dominant instrument, typically administered from both client and clinician perspectives. Observer-rated instruments such as CALPAS (Andrusyna et al., 2001) offer an alternative to self-report, addressing some — though not all — concerns about shared-method bias between alliance and outcome measures, both of which are commonly client-reported. Session-by-session alliance tracking is methodologically possible but resource-intensive, and most published research instead measures alliance at one or a small number of timepoints across a course of treatment lasting months.

### Proposed Mechanisms

No single mechanism is established as causal. Candidate explanations include: alliance creating sufficient safety for psychological exploration (psychoanalytic tradition); alliance enabling adherence to between-session tasks such as homework (cognitive-behavioral tradition); alliance as the therapeutic factor itself, not merely a facilitator of one (humanistic tradition); and a more recent, still-developing proposal that alliance reflects a regulated nervous system state that enables learning and change (an extension of polyvagal and interpersonal-neurobiology frameworks; Porges, 2011; Siegel, 2012). None of these mechanisms is mutually exclusive, and none has been isolated experimentally with enough precision to rule out the others.

### Why This Literature Cannot Simply Be Imported

Four features of the psychotherapy alliance literature limit its direct applicability to manual therapy:

1. It is built almost entirely on extended courses of weekly treatment, not brief or single-session encounters.
2. It is built almost entirely on verbal, not physical, clinical contact.
3. Its instruments assume a self-report of relational agreement is the right unit of measurement; whether this captures anything relevant in a context where the primary clinical act is physical handling, rather than conversation, is untested.
4. Its proposed mechanisms are predominantly psychological (insight, adherence, cognitive reframing), not somatic — even where nervous-system framing has been proposed, it has not been operationalized or tested in this literature.

These are not reasons to dismiss the psychotherapy alliance literature. They are reasons it cannot be assumed to transfer without verification.

## 3. What We Know About Alliance in Manual Therapy

The literature specific to manual therapy is considerably thinner, more fragmented across professions, and rarely uses alliance-specific instrumentation.

### Physical Therapy

A modest and growing literature links therapeutic relationship quality to adherence and outcome in physical therapy (Spence-Jones, 2004; May et al., 2010). Clinician communication and perceived warmth correlate with client satisfaction. However, few studies use a validated alliance instrument (e.g., the WAI) rather than satisfaction or adherence as a proxy, making it difficult to compare effect sizes directly with the psychotherapy literature.

### Massage Therapy

Research specifically addressing alliance in massage therapy is sparse. Client satisfaction correlates with outcome, and clinician presence and communication appear to matter (Field, 2010; Lowe, 2009), but no standardized alliance measurement has been applied to this population. Touch quality is frequently invoked as relevant to client experience, but the relationship between touch quality and alliance, specifically, has not been isolated.

### Chiropractic Care

Patient satisfaction predicts adherence and outcome in chiropractic care, and the therapeutic relationship is often established within a single visit. As with massage therapy, satisfaction functions as a proxy for alliance rather than alliance being measured directly, and the rapid timeline of relationship formation in this setting has not been studied as an alliance-formation question per se.

### Other Hands-On Modalities

Osteopathy, athletic training, and related disciplines have even less direct evidence. Clinical observation broadly supports the relevance of relationship quality, but rigorous alliance research is largely absent.

### Emerging, Cross-Modality Themes

Despite the thinness of modality-specific evidence, several patterns recur across the manual therapy literature:

1. Relationship quality appears to form quickly — within one to three sessions — in brief-encounter modalities.
2. Clinician communication and presence appear to establish trust rapidly.
3. Touch quality is repeatedly cited as relevant to client perception of care and to outcome, though rarely measured directly against alliance.
4. Functional improvement, not psychological insight, is the outcome of primary interest.
5. Client perception of safety appears central to satisfaction and adherence, though "safety" in this literature is rarely defined in nervous-system terms.
6. Client autonomy and the ability to decline or modify treatment are implicit in clinical practice but are almost never studied explicitly as an alliance-relevant variable.

### Why the Manual Therapy Alliance Literature Is Thin

Several structural factors plausibly explain this gap. Manual therapy disciplines have historically emphasized technique efficacy as the primary research priority, with relationship factors treated as secondary or unmeasured. Existing alliance instruments were built for, and validated in, talk-based psychotherapy and do not map cleanly onto a clinical encounter organized around physical handling. Brief encounters make the kind of longitudinal, multi-timepoint alliance tracking common in psychotherapy research logistically difficult. And touch introduces a measurement problem that talk-based therapy does not have: it is unclear what an analogue to verbal "rapport" should even look like when the primary clinical act is manual contact, and how (or whether) physiological measures should supplement self-report in that context.

## 4. How Manual Therapy Alliance May Differ From Psychotherapy Alliance

The following is a set of open questions, not established findings. Each names a structural difference between the two clinical contexts and the empirical question that difference raises. None of these differences has been demonstrated to produce a mechanistically distinct form of alliance; that is precisely what remains untested.

**Time scale.** Psychotherapy alliance research describes a relationship developing over weeks to months of repeated weekly contact. Manual therapy alliance, if it exists as a comparable construct, may need to form within one to three sessions. *Open question*: does a brief encounter activate the same alliance-relevant processes as an extended relationship, only faster — or is something different happening?

**Embodied contact.** Psychotherapy alliance is relational and predominantly verbal. Manual therapy alliance, if such a construct is meaningful, is established substantially through touch. *Open question*: does the channel through which the relationship is conveyed (touch vs. words) change how alliance is perceived, formed, or functions — or is touch simply another channel through which the same relational processes operate?

**Functional vs. insight goals.** Psychotherapy is oriented toward psychological insight and change; manual therapy is oriented toward functional and physical outcomes (movement, pain). *Open question*: does alliance serve the same role in the causal pathway to outcome when the target outcome is functional rather than psychological?

**Touch as the primary clinical act.** In psychotherapy, touch is minimal or absent; in manual therapy, touch is the intervention itself, not an adjunct to it. *Open question*: does skilled touch communicate something about competence and safety that words cannot, or is this a difference of degree rather than kind?

**Possible role of nervous system co-regulation.** Some authors have proposed that alliance reflects, at least in part, a regulated autonomic state achieved through the clinician-client interaction (Porges, 2011; Siegel, 2012). This proposal predates manual therapy-specific research and has not been tested with physiological measures in this context. *Open question*: if nervous system co-regulation is part of how alliance operates, is it more measurable, or more central, in a touch-based clinical encounter than in a verbal one — and what would distinguish this from co-regulation processes already described in psychotherapy (where clinician tone, pacing, and presence are also thought to matter)?

**Permission and autonomy.** In psychotherapy, respect for client autonomy is largely implicit. In manual therapy, the clinician makes repeated, moment-to-moment decisions that directly touch the client's body — *can I work here? is this pressure acceptable? continue or stop?* — making the client's felt ability to decline a recurring, practical question rather than a background ethical assumption. *Open question*: does this make a client's felt permission to decline a more salient, more frequently tested, and possibly more outcome-relevant variable in manual therapy than in talk-based therapy — or is this simply autonomy support (Self-Determination Theory; Deci & Ryan) under a different label, operating identically in both contexts? We take no position on this question in this paper; it is the subject of Paper 2 in this sequence.

## 5. Specific Research Gaps

Seven concrete, addressable gaps follow from the comparison above.

**Gap 1 — Alliance measurement in manual therapy.** Existing instruments (WAI, CALPAS) were built for and validated in psychotherapy. It is unknown whether they can be meaningfully adapted to brief, touch-based encounters, or whether manual-therapy-specific instruments are needed. It is also unknown whether any physiological marker (e.g., heart rate variability) corresponds to client-reported alliance in this context, or whether self-report and physiology will turn out to be measuring different things.

**Gap 2 — Brief-encounter alliance formation.** How alliance forms differently across a 1-hour single encounter versus a 50-week relationship is essentially unstudied. Whether alliance can be meaningfully measured at the end of a single session, and what predicts faster or slower formation, remains open.

**Gap 3 — Touch as communication.** What specific qualities of touch (pressure, pacing, contact consistency) affect client perception of safety or competence is not established. Whether physiological markers change in response to touch quality, independent of verbal communication, has not been tested in manual therapy populations.

**Gap 4 — Possible nervous system correlates of alliance.** Whether autonomic markers (e.g., HRV, vagal tone indices) correlate with client-reported alliance in manual therapy is untested. This question must be approached carefully: as the Sprint 002B adversarial review of this project noted, "nervous system safety" is intuitively appealing but currently lacks an operational definition independent of the self-report it is meant to explain. Any research program addressing this gap must specify, in advance, which physiological markers are predicted to change and why — to avoid circularity in which any observed physiological pattern is post-hoc relabeled as evidence of "safety."

**Gap 5 — Permission as a distinct factor.** Whether client-felt permission to decline or modify treatment is distinguishable, in measurement and in effect on outcome, from existing constructs — informed consent, shared decision-making, autonomy support — is unresolved. This question is the explicit subject of Paper 2 in this sequence and is not adjudicated here.

**Gap 6 — Modality comparison.** Whether alliance (or its functional analogue) operates identically across physical therapy, massage, chiropractic, and osteopathy, or whether modality-specific mechanisms exist, has not been tested directly.

**Gap 7 — Clinician training effects.** Whether alliance-relevant skills can be taught, and whether training produces measurable change in either alliance ratings or clinical outcome, has not been tested in manual therapy populations specifically (though analogous training research exists in psychotherapy and in communication-skills training more broadly).

## 6. Proposed Research Agenda

### Phase 1 — Foundational Research (Year 1–2)

1. **Measurement development.** Adapt or develop an alliance measure appropriate to brief-encounter manual therapy; validate it against functional outcomes across at least two modalities. Identify, in advance, candidate physiological markers to test for correlation with self-reported alliance, rather than searching post-hoc for any marker that correlates.
2. **Mechanism exploration.** Conduct qualitative research on what clients in manual therapy actually describe as relevant to trust and safety, prior to assuming the psychotherapy alliance construct transfers intact. Pair this with quantitative tracking of how quickly relationship-relevant ratings stabilize across sessions.
3. **Literature synthesis.** Conduct a formal systematic review (this paper is explicitly not one) of relationship factors and manual therapy outcomes, and a synthesis of touch and nervous-system literatures as they may apply to this context.

### Phase 2 — Testing Specific Mechanisms (Year 2–3)

4. **Permission effects.** A randomized comparison of an explicit permission-focused communication approach against usual care, measuring client-reported safety to decline, functional outcome, and (where feasible) physiological markers. Designed to address Gap 5 directly.
5. **Clinician training.** A pre/post intervention study testing whether training clinicians in specific communication and presence skills changes alliance ratings and/or outcomes.
6. **Modality comparison.** A comparative study of relationship-formation timelines and correlates across physical therapy, massage, and chiropractic, testing whether a single mechanism or modality-specific mechanisms better explain the data.

### Phase 3 — Framework Testing (Year 3+)

7. **Integrated framework testing.** Once Phases 1–2 produce results, test whether an integrated model (combining communication, context, touch, and autonomy factors) predicts outcome better than any single factor alone, and better than competing explanations such as a pure-expectation or pure-technique-skill model (see Section 4 of the Sprint 002B adversarial review for the full statement of these competing frameworks).

This agenda is deliberately sequenced so that later phases depend on, rather than assume, the findings of earlier phases. We do not propose testing the full "Clinical Reasoning Architecture" synthesis (Paper 3 in this sequence) until the foundational measurement and mechanism questions in Phases 1–2 have been addressed.

## 7. Implications

### For Clinical Practice

Manual therapists currently have no validated way to assess whether their relationship with a client is contributing to outcome, distinct from assessing client satisfaction. Until Gap 1 is addressed, claims about "building alliance" in manual therapy training rest on extrapolation from psychotherapy, not on direct evidence. Clinicians should treat relationship-building practices imported from psychotherapy training as plausible, not proven, in this context.

### For Research

This is a tractable, underinvestigated area. The relevant instruments, theoretical frameworks, and physiological measurement tools already exist in adjacent literatures (psychotherapy, polyvagal research, pain science); what is missing is their deliberate, careful application — with appropriate skepticism — to the manual therapy context.

### For Education

If subsequent research (Phases 1–2 above) confirms that specific communication and presence skills affect outcome in manual therapy, this has direct implications for how curricula are structured. At present, this remains a hypothesis to test, not a basis for curriculum change.

### For Future Work

This paper is the foundation paper in a five-paper sequence (see [[PUBLICATION_STRATEGY.md]]). Paper 2 addresses the permission question raised in Gap 5 directly. Paper 3 proposes an integrated clinical reasoning framework — but only after Papers 1–2 establish the evidentiary basis for it. We flag explicitly that the integration described in Paper 3 is not assumed by this paper; it is a separate claim requiring its own justification.

## 8. Conclusion

The therapeutic alliance is one of the best-replicated findings in psychotherapy outcome research, but that research was built in a clinical context — extended, verbal, insight-oriented — that differs from manual therapy in several structural ways: time scale, primary medium, and outcome target. Whether alliance, as currently understood, transfers to brief, touch-based, function-oriented clinical encounters is an open empirical question, not a settled one. We have identified seven specific gaps in the current literature and proposed a three-phase research agenda to address them, beginning with the measurement problem that underlies all subsequent questions.

Manual therapists deliver an enormous volume of direct clinical contact every year. Whether, and how, relationship quality contributes to the outcomes of that contact deserves the same rigor applied to it in psychotherapy research over the past four decades — no more, and no less. We have deliberately avoided concluding, in this paper, that manual therapy alliance is biologically or mechanistically distinct from psychotherapy alliance. That claim, raised by some of the open questions in Section 4, is exactly what the proposed research agenda is designed to test.

We invite manual therapy researchers to pursue the measurement and mechanism questions raised here; clinicians to participate in this research and to treat imported alliance-building practices as plausible rather than proven; educators to await evidence before restructuring curricula around relationship-building content; and funding bodies to recognize this as a tractable, currently underinvested area of clinical research.
