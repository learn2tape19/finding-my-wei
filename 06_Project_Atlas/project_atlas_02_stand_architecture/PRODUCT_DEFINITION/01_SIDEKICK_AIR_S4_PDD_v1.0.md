## Document Purpose

This document defines **what** the Sidekick Air Structural Support System (S4) must accomplish.

It intentionally **avoids prescribing how** engineering solutions should be achieved.

Engineering partners are expected to determine optimal technical implementations while satisfying these requirements.

---

## Vision

> Create the world's most portable professional-grade therapeutic support platform capable of delivering the stability, confidence, and performance of a traditional treatment table while fitting into airline luggage.

The Structural Support System is a critical subsystem of the broader Sidekick Air ecosystem. It is the load-bearing architecture upon which future products will be built. This document defines that architecture.

---

## Mission

Engineer a deployable structural platform that professionals trust with another human being.

The system must inspire confidence before treatment begins.

## Success Definition

Users consistently describe the platform as:

✓ **Stable** — No rocking, no deflection under load, trustworthy  
✓ **Professional** — Appearance suitable for clinical/medical settings  
✓ **Lightweight** — Portable for mobile practitioners  
✓ **Fast** — Rapid deployment (<60 seconds preferred)  
✓ **Trustworthy** — Inspires confidence before use  
✓ **Durable** — Years of daily use without failure  
✓ **Easy to Deploy** — Intuitive, one-person setup, no tools  

If all seven attributes are achieved, the product succeeds. Missing any one damages market viability.

## Functional Requirements

### The System Shall:

1. **Securely support the inflatable platform** — Must interface cleanly with current and future inflatable designs

2. **Distribute load evenly** — No stress concentration; forces transmitted smoothly through structure

3. **Minimize structural deflection** — User should perceive no sagging or deflection under typical load

4. **Prevent rocking** — Four-point contact (or equivalent) prevents wobbling on uneven terrain

5. **Resist torsional movement** — Structure remains rigid when rotated or twisted

6. **Deploy rapidly** — Full setup in <90 seconds (preferred: <60 seconds), no tools required

7. **Collapse quickly** — Full collapse and pack into transport case in <2 minutes

8. **Require no tools** — One person, bare hands, no wrenches/screwdrivers/adapters

9. **Fit within airline travel constraints** — Dimensions meet TSA/airline checked baggage limits (~62" linear dimension)

10. **Perform on uneven terrain** — Operates safely on grass, concrete, floors, inclines, outdoor surfaces

11. **Support future accessory integration** — Architecture allows mounting of future accessories (arm supports, mobilization tools, extensors)

12. **Interface cleanly with the inflatable platform** — Minimal connection points, clean aesthetic, tool-free interface

## Performance Targets

### Weight

- **Preferred:** ≤18 lbs
- **Maximum Acceptable:** 22 lbs
- **Rationale:** Portability for mobile practitioners; comparable to professional massage table weight

### Static Working Load

- **Minimum:** 800+ lbs
- **Recommended:** 1000 lbs
- **Rationale:** Support 200+ lb clinician + 200+ lb patient + dynamic load factors

### Deployment Speed

- **Preferred:** <60 seconds
- **Acceptable:** <90 seconds
- **Rationale:** Clinical workflow requires fast setup between clients

### Collapse Speed

- **Target:** <2 minutes
- **Rationale:** Fits typical clinic transition time

### Service Life

- **Minimum:** 10+ years
- **Outdoor Capability:** Yes (UV, moisture, temperature resistant)
- **Medical-Grade Cleanability:** Yes (compatible with hospital-grade disinfectants)

### Stability

- **Maximum Deflection Under 800 lb Load:** <0.25 inches
- **No Rocking on Uneven Terrain:** Acceptable surface variation ±2 inches
- **Torsional Rigidity:** Minimal twist when rotational force applied

### Durability

- **Deployment Cycles:** 10,000+ cycles (equivalent to 5 years heavy use)
- **Outdoor Durability:** 5+ years continuous outdoor exposure
- **Wear Components:** Feet, hinges, latches replaceable without structural rebuild

## Structural Objectives

### Design Goals

- **High Torsional Rigidity** — Minimal twist under rotational forces
- **Minimal Deflection** — <0.25" under 800 lb static load
- **Balanced Load Distribution** — No local stress concentrations
- **Long Fatigue Life** — 10,000+ deployment cycles without failure
- **Replaceable Wear Components** — Feet, hinges, latches replaceable
- **Field Serviceability** — Repairs/replacements without specialized equipment
- **Material Efficiency** — Optimized for weight without sacrificing confidence

### Confidence-Based Design Philosophy

Structural design must prioritize user confidence and safety over minimum weight.

A slightly heavier platform that inspires absolute confidence outperforms a lightweight platform that creates doubt.

Example: If 1 lb of additional material increases structural confidence significantly, the material trade-off is justified.

## Environmental Requirements

### Operating Environments

The system must safely operate in:

- **Indoor:** Climate-controlled therapy clinics
- **Outdoor:** Grass fields, athletic facilities
- **Hard Surfaces:** Concrete, tile, gym floors
- **Uneven Terrain:** Natural ground, sand, gravel (±2" variation)
- **Temperature:** -10°F to +100°F operational range
- **Humidity:** 10% to 90% relative humidity
- **Weather Exposure:** Light rain during deployment; not waterproof, but weather-resistant
- **Clinical Environment:** Compatible with hospital-grade disinfectants (bleach, isopropyl alcohol, quaternary ammonium)
- **UV Exposure:** If used outdoors continuously, materials must resist UV degradation

### Cleanliness & Hygiene

- Medical-grade cleanability (surfaces must tolerate harsh cleaning)
- No crevices where bacteria can hide
- Smooth surfaces for easy wipe-down
- Drainage (no water pooling in crevices)
- Compatibility with sterilization (if medical-grade use required)

## Manufacturing Objectives

### Manufacturability

- **Scalable** — Design must transition from prototype (1-10 units) through low volume (100-1000) to medium volume (10,000+)
- **Repeatable** — Consistent quality across all units
- **Serviceable** — Field repairs and component replacement without full rebuild
- **Sustainable** — Minimal waste, recyclable components

### Design for Manufacturing (DFM)

- Minimize part count (fewer parts = lower cost, faster assembly)
- Standard materials (off-the-shelf components where possible)
- Consistent manufacturing tolerances (not requiring precision tooling)
- Parallel assembly processes (subsystems built independently, assembled at end)

### Design for Assembly (DFA)

- Clear assembly sequence (logical, intuitive order)
- Minimal tools required (preferably no tools)
- Error-proof assembly (parts fit only correct way)
- Repeatability (assembly time consistent across units)

### Future Automation Compatibility

- Design should allow for future manufacturing automation
- Component geometries should be automation-friendly (robots can handle them)
- Don't over-engineer for hand assembly if automation will eventually replace it

## Preferred Materials

The following materials are preferred pending engineering evaluation:

### Primary Structure

- **Aluminum Alloys** (6061-T6, 7075-T6) — Light, strong, corrosion-resistant
- **Composite Structures** (carbon fiber, fiberglass) — Lightweight, high strength-to-weight
- **Carbon Fiber** (where justified by weight or performance) — Premium option; evaluate cost/benefit

### Secondary Components

- **Engineering Plastics** (nylon, polycarbonate, polyurethane) — Hinges, latches, feet
- **Rubber** (natural or synthetic) — Feet, vibration damping
- **Stainless Steel** — Hardware (bolts, rivets, hinges)

### Material Selection Remains Open

This list is **preferred**, not **mandatory**. Engineering partners should evaluate:

- Weight vs. cost trade-offs
- Manufacturing feasibility
- Environmental compatibility
- Long-term durability
- Supply chain stability
- Recyclability

Novel materials should be proposed if they offer significant advantages.

## Safety Philosophy

### Structural Confidence is a Product Requirement

Users should never question whether another human being is safely supported.

The system must inspire absolute confidence in:
- Load capacity
- Stability
- Durability
- Reliability

### Design Principle

If there is any doubt about safety, the design is not acceptable.

Example: If engineers question whether 800 lbs is sufficient load capacity, the design should target higher until confidence is absolute.

---

## Cost Philosophy

### Cost Optimization Never Compromises Structural Confidence

Manufacturing cost reductions are welcome, but not at the expense of:
- Safety
- Durability
- User confidence
- Reliability

Example: If reducing cost requires eliminating redundant structural members, and that creates even slight doubt about safety, the cost reduction is not acceptable.

## Unknowns

The following engineering questions remain **unresolved**. They are **engineering investigations**, not product requirements:

### Mechanism Design

- **Folding mechanism** — How does the structure fold? (Hinges? Pivots? Telescoping? Collapsing frame?)
- **Locking system** — How are joints locked in deployed state? (Mechanical latches? Friction locks? Spring locks?)
- **Deployment actuation** — What causes deployment? (Gravity? Manual push? Spring assist? Active motor?)
- **Release mechanism** — How is the structure released for collapse? (Button? Lever? Push-pin?)

### Geometry & Form Factor

- **Number of legs** — 3-point? 4-point? More? (Affects stability and weight)
- **Transport geometry** — Compact folded dimensions? Luggage-friendly? What are the constraints?
- **Height adjustment** — Fixed height? Adjustable? Motorized? (Affects complexity and weight)
- **Width and length** — Do they change? Are they modular?

### Material Selection

- **Aluminum vs. composite** — Which offers best weight/cost/durability balance?
- **Specific alloys** — 6061? 7075? Carbon fiber? Hybrid approach?
- **Fastener strategy** — Bolts? Rivets? Welded? Adhesive?

### Manufacturing Strategy

- **Production method** — Welded aluminum? Molded composite? Assembly of components?
- **Tooling investment** — How much tooling is required? When does ROI happen?
- **Supplier strategy** — Make in-house? Contract manufacturing? Hybrid?
- **Scaling path** — How does production scale from 10 to 10,000 units?

### Integration Details

- **Connection to inflatable** — Bolts? Adhesive? Magnetic? Tool-free latches?
- **Accessory mounting** — Where do accessories attach? How are loads transferred?
- **Transport case** — What case design? Wheeled? Backpack-style?

### Testing & Validation

- **Load testing protocol** — Static? Dynamic? Fatigue? Environmental?
- **Deployment cycle testing** — How many cycles before acceptance?
- **Field testing locations** — Which clinics, environments, users?
- **Clinical trial requirements** — Is regulatory approval needed?

These questions are **legitimate engineering investigations**, not deficiencies in the Product Definition.

Engineering partners will resolve these questions through technical analysis, prototyping, and testing.

## Guiding Principle

> The objective is **not** to engineer a folding stand.
>
> The objective is to engineer a **deployable structural subsystem** that becomes the **foundation of the Sidekick Air ecosystem**.

The S4 will support:
- Current therapeutic uses
- Future medical applications
- Future military applications
- Future accessory ecosystem
- Future variants (stretcher, wider platform, higher load capacity)

Design decisions should be made with this long-term ecosystem vision in mind.

A stand optimized only for current massage therapy use may create constraints that prevent future expansion.
