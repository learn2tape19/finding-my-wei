## The One Thing That Changes Everything

The difference between an AI assistant that feels useful and one that feels like a fancy search engine is context.

When you give your agent real context -- what you're actually trying to accomplish, what you've already tried, what's at stake -- the quality of what you get back goes up dramatically. The more you treat it like a conversation with a smart colleague and less like a Google query, the better this gets.

That's it. That's the main thing.

## The SOUL.md File

The file called `SOUL.md` (included) is what shapes your agent's personality and how it approaches problems. Right now it's set up as a general business partner. As you figure out what you actually use your agent for, you can update it to be more specific.

If you ever want to change how the agent communicates, what it focuses on, or how it handles specific situations -- that's the file to edit. Think of it as the ongoing definition of your working relationship.

## How to Get Good Results Fast

**Be specific about what you want.** "Research the portable massage table market" gets you a lot of information. "Research the portable massage table market -- I'm specifically trying to understand what professional therapists say they hate about current options and whether that matches what customers say" gets you something you can actually use.

**Tell it what success looks like.** "Give me a one-page summary I could use in a sales conversation" is more useful than just asking for research.

**Give it permission to push back.** If your agent just agrees with everything, you're not getting value. A good AI partner tells you when something doesn't add up. The SOUL.md is written to encourage this -- but you can reinforce it by asking "what am I missing?" or "what's the argument against this?"

**Use it for thinking, not just tasks.** Some of the most valuable conversations you'll have are the ones where you're just thinking through a decision. Bring it a problem you're stuck on and talk through it. You might find that explaining it out loud surfaces the answer, or the agent asks a question you hadn't considered.

## Things to Try First

A few starting points for Sidekick Air specifically:

1. **Market positioning gut-check.** Describe how you currently position Sidekick Air -- who it's for, what makes it different. Ask your agent to push back and offer alternative framings. Useful for stress-testing your messaging.

2. **Customer review mining.** Ask it to help you think through what your customers actually say they value versus what you think they value. Good for spotting gaps.

3. **Competitive landscape.** "Help me understand the competitive landscape for portable massage tables -- who are the main players, how do they position themselves, and where are the gaps?"

4. **A specific decision you've been sitting on.** Bring a real business decision you've been thinking about. Use the agent to think through the tradeoffs. See if talking it through helps.

## What Your Agent Can't Do

To save you some frustration:

- It can't take actions in the real world (send emails, make purchases, update your systems) unless you've set that up specifically.
- It doesn't have real-time information unless you've enabled web search tools.
- It doesn't remember previous conversations unless you build in a memory system (something to explore later).
- It can be confidently wrong. Always apply your own judgment, especially on things that matter.

## The Model Question

If your agent feels dry and overly cautious (which is common with Codex 5.3), switching models makes a big difference. Sean has had much better results with models tuned for conversation rather than pure coding. Worth exploring with whoever set up your OpenClaw instance.

## One Last Thing

The agents that become genuinely useful are the ones where the human invests in the relationship. Not dramatically -- just by being willing to have real conversations instead of just issuing commands.

The first few sessions are about figuring out how to work together. Don't judge it too quickly. Try different kinds of tasks. Push back when it gives you something generic. Ask follow-up questions. The groove takes a little while to find, but once you do, it changes how you work.
