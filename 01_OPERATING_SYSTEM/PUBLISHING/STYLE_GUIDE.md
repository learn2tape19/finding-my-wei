# Style Guide

**Authority:** Editorial Board

**Applies To:** All Finding My Wei publications

---

## Paragraph Standards

### Length
- **Maximum:** 150 words
- **Preferred:** 50-100 words
- **Minimum:** 1-2 sentences is acceptable if it makes a complete point

### Structure
- One primary idea per paragraph
- Supporting details follow the main idea
- Transitions between paragraphs should be smooth but not required to be explicit
- Short paragraphs create rhythm and readability

### Spacing
- Double line break between paragraphs
- Single line break between heading and first paragraph
- Generous white space throughout

---

## Heading Hierarchy

### H1 — Article or Document Title
- Used once at the very beginning
- Should be descriptive and clear
- Example: "Safety Is the First Intervention"

### H2 — Major Sections
- Divide content into major topics
- Should be specific enough to guide readers
- Example: "Why Relationship Matters More Than Technique"

### H3 — Subsections
- Organize content within major sections
- Clarify the argument or structure
- Example: "The Role of Permission"

### H4 — Minor Points (Sparingly)
- Only use when necessary for clarity
- Avoid excessive heading levels

**Rule:** No clever, vague, or overly cute headings. Let headings be clear signposts.

---

## Sentence Rhythm

Vary sentence length to create rhythm:

- **Short sentences:** Create emphasis and clarity
- **Medium sentences:** Carry the main argument
- **Long sentences:** Can hold complex ideas, but keep them readable

Avoid:

- All short sentences (becomes choppy)
- All long sentences (becomes exhausting to read)
- Overly complex clause structures

Read sentences aloud. They should flow naturally.

---

## Formatting Standards

### Bold
- Use to emphasize key concepts or definitions
- Not for ordinary text
- Example: "**Therapeutic alliance** is the foundation..."

### Italics
- Use for emphasis when bold is too strong
- Use for introducing terms: *neuroception*
- Use sparingly

### Lists

#### Bulleted Lists
Use when items are not sequential and not hierarchical:

- First item
- Second item
- Third item

#### Numbered Lists
Use only when sequence or priority matters:

1. First step
2. Second step
3. Third step

#### Definition Lists
Use for terms and meanings:

**Term:** Definition or explanation of the term and why it matters.

### Quotations

#### In-Text Quotations
Keep them short. Never quote a full paragraph.

> "Short, meaningful quotes illuminate the text."

#### Block Quotations
Use for longer quotes that deserve special attention.

Introduce the quote with context. Explain why it matters to your point.

Quote only the essential words. Never include unnecessary surrounding text.

### Capitalization

- Capitalize proper nouns and names
- Capitalize the first word of sentences
- Do NOT capitalize common words for emphasis
- Titles follow AP style (capitalize first, last, and major words)

### Spacing

- Single space within paragraphs
- Double space between paragraphs
- Single space after periods in prose (not two spaces)

## Markdown Standards

### Headers
```
# H1 — Article Title
## H2 — Major Section
### H3 — Subsection
#### H4 — Minor Point (rarely used)
```

### Bold
```
**bold text**
```

### Italics
```
*italic text*
```

### Lists
```

- Another point

2. Second numbered item

```

### Block Quotes
```

```

### Horizontal Rule
```

```

Use sparingly to separate major sections.

## Quotation Marks

- Use "smart quotes" (curly quotes) not "straight quotes"
- Single quotes for quotes within quotes: "He said, 'hello.'"
- Periods and commas go inside the closing quotation mark
- Semicolons and colons go outside: "example"; "example": example

---

## Apostrophes

- Use smart apostrophes (curly) not straight apostrophes
- Contractions: it's, don't, shouldn't
- Possessives: the clinic's location, the clinicians' observations

---

## Oxford Comma

Use the Oxford comma (the comma before "and" in a series):

Good: "The nervous system, the musculature, and the psychology all interact."

Avoid: "The nervous system, the musculature and the psychology all interact."

---

## Hyphen vs. Em Dash vs. Semicolon

### Hyphen (-)
- Connects compound words: "well-being," "long-term"
- Connects words that form a single modifier: "evidence-based practice"

### Semicolon (;)
- Connects two related independent clauses: "The evidence is clear; clinicians recognize this pattern."
- Separates items in a complex list

### Em Dash
**NEVER USE EM DASHES.**

Replace with:
- A comma
- A semicolon
- A new sentence

---

## Ellipsis

Use ellipses (…) sparingly to indicate omitted words:

"To observe... is to understand."

Do not use to trail off in prose. Use a period instead.

---

## Numbers

- Spell out numbers under ten in text: "five patients," "three observations"
- Use numerals for numbers ten and above: "14 studies," "96% reported"
- Use numerals for statistical information: "75% of clinicians"
- Spell out numbers at the beginning of sentences: "Fifty-three patients participated..."

Exception: Years are always numerals: "2026"

---

## Abbreviations

Introduce abbreviations with full term, then abbreviation in parentheses:

"The central nervous system (CNS) controls..."

Thereafter use the abbreviation.

---

## Consistency

### Terminology
Use the same term consistently throughout. Don't alternate between synonyms unless intentional.

Example:
- Choose "nervous system" or "nervous system"
- Choose "patient" or "client" or "person" — but use it consistently

### Verb Tense
Use present tense for general truths and observations:

"Clinicians notice this pattern consistently."

Use past tense for specific events or studies:

"The study showed..."

### Tone
Maintain the institutional voice throughout. Don't shift between formal and casual.

---

## Length Standards

### Sentence Length
- Aim for 10-25 words per sentence
- Vary from 5 to 40 words for rhythm
- Anything over 40 words should be rare and carefully crafted

### Paragraph Length
- 50-100 words is ideal
- Never exceed 150 words
- 1-2 sentences is fine if complete

### Article Length
- Short articles: 800-1,200 words
- Medium articles: 1,200-2,500 words
- Long articles: 2,500-4,000 words

Length should serve the content, not constrain it.

---

## Line Breaks and Spacing

### Within Section
Double line break between paragraphs. Single line break is not sufficient.

### Between Major Sections
Triple line break (or use ---) to create visual separation.

### Within Lists
Single line break between bullet points.

---

## Final Standards

These standards serve readability and voice.

When a rule conflicts with clarity or good writing, clarity wins.

Editorial Board reviews each publication for consistency.

Standards improve with each publication.
