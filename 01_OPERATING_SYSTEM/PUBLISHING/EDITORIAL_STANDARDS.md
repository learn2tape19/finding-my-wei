## Purpose

This document establishes the permanent editorial standards for every piece of writing produced by Finding My Wei.

These standards are cumulative. Every editorial improvement discovered through publication, peer review, reader feedback, or Founder input becomes part of this document.

The institution should continuously improve its writing.

The same editorial lesson should never require correction twice.

---

## Editorial Philosophy

**The purpose of our writing is not persuasion.**

**The purpose is understanding.**

We do not write to convince.

We write to help readers observe more deeply.

Readers should arrive at insight rather than being pushed toward conclusions.

The writing should create discovery.

Never argument.

Not a marketer.

Not a salesperson.

Not an academic trying to impress.

**Quiet confidence. Clinical humility. Intellectual curiosity. Respect for uncertainty. Calm authority.**

## Fundamental Editorial Principles

### 1. Curiosity Before Certainty

Avoid absolute statements whenever possible.

Instead of declaring conclusions, invite exploration.

**Avoid:**
- This proves...
- This is why...
- Always...
- Never...
- Everything changes...

**Instead prefer:**
- We often observe...
- One possible explanation is...
- It invites us to consider...
- Many clinicians notice...
- This may help explain...

Clinical confidence should never require exaggerated certainty.

### 2. Distinguish Evidence From Interpretation

Clearly separate:

1. Established evidence
2. Current scientific understanding
3. Clinical observation
4. Professional interpretation
5. Personal reflection

Readers should always know which level they are reading.

Never present interpretation as established scientific fact.

### 3. Scientific Precision

Avoid overstating neuroscience.

Avoid reducing complex biological systems to simple explanations.

**Examples to avoid:**
- Your vagal tone affects your patient's vagal tone.
- Pain decreases because...
- The nervous system makes...
- Everything is neuroplasticity.

**Instead write:**
- Autonomic state may influence...
- Neuroception appears to contribute...
- Current research suggests...
- Clinical observation indicates...

Protect scientific credibility above dramatic language.

### 4. Relationship Before Technique

Technique matters.

Relationship influences how technique is experienced.

**Presence. Permission. Safety. Trust. Expectation. Collaboration. Attention.**

These are not separate concepts.

They are different expressions of therapeutic alliance.

Every article should reinforce this principle naturally.

Never prioritize technique over relationship.

### 5. Presence Before Explanation

Good educational writing follows this sequence:

1. Observation
2. Story
3. Question
4. Reflection
5. Science
6. Clinical application

Do not begin with theory.

Begin with experience.

### 6. Reflection Before Instruction

Readers should never feel lectured.

Reduce directive language.

**Avoid:**
- Do this.
- You should...
- Always...

**Instead ask:**
- What happens if...
- What might you notice...
- Could this explain...
- How would your next patient experience this...

Invite reflection.

Do not prescribe behavior.

### 7. Write Like a Clinician

This is perhaps the most important rule.

Every article should sound like a conversation with a trusted colleague after a long day in clinic.

Never sound like content marketing.

Never sound like social media optimization.

Never sound like advertising.

Never sound like an AI trying to persuade.

The reader should feel respected.

### 8. Quiet Confidence

Avoid unnecessary intensity.

Reduce words such as:

- groundbreaking
- revolutionary
- transformative
- game changing
- incredible
- amazing
- perfect
- mastery
- ultimate
- unlock
- hack

Instead allow the ideas to carry the weight.

Confidence comes from clarity.

Not volume.

### 9. Respect Complexity

Human beings are complex.

Avoid simplistic explanations.

Allow room for uncertainty.

Good clinicians recognize nuance.

Our writing should do the same.

### 10. Therapeutic Alliance Is the Thread

Whenever appropriate, reinforce the central idea:

**Clinical change occurs within relationship.**

Everything else expands from that principle.

- Presence
- Safety
- Permission
- Touch
- Communication
- Expectation
- Context
- Clinical reasoning

Each article should illuminate one facet of the same larger picture.

## Story Structure

Whenever possible:

1. Begin with a real clinical moment
2. Allow readers to recognize themselves
3. Create curiosity
4. Only then introduce scientific discussion

Avoid beginning with explanation.

## Paragraph Structure

Keep paragraphs short.

One idea per paragraph.

Allow generous white space.

Writing should breathe.

The pacing should mirror the calm presence we advocate.

**Maximum paragraph length:** 150 words

**Preferred paragraph length:** 50-100 words

Short paragraphs create rhythm and allow readers to absorb ideas.

---

## Punctuation Standards

See PUNCTUATION_STANDARD.md for comprehensive guidance.

Most critical rule:

**EM DASHES ARE PROHIBITED.**

Anywhere.

Replace every em dash with:

- a comma,
- a semicolon;
- or
- a new sentence.

This is now a permanent Finding My Wei editorial standard.

---

## Headings and Structure

Use clear, hierarchical headings.

Each heading should indicate the content of the section it introduces.

Avoid clever or vague heading language.

Let headings be clear signposts for readers.

## Editorial Review Checklist

Before approving any publication, verify:

### Voice & Tone
- ✓ Sounds like Finding My Wei
- ✓ Reflective rather than persuasive
- ✓ Clinically grounded
- ✓ Sounds like an experienced clinician

### Scientific Integrity
- ✓ Scientifically precise
- ✓ No exaggerated certainty
- ✓ No unsupported neuroscience
- ✓ Evidence clearly distinguished from interpretation

### Structure & Flow
- ✓ Story before explanation
- ✓ Observation before conclusion
- ✓ Reflection before instruction
- ✓ Strong opening that creates curiosity
- ✓ Memorable closing that invites further thinking

### Craft & Mechanics
- ✓ Short paragraphs
- ✓ Clear headings
- ✓ White space
- ✓ Calm pacing
- ✓ Consistent terminology
- ✓ No AI clichés
- ✓ No em dashes
- ✓ Proper punctuation

### Institutional Quality
- ✓ Worth preserving ten years from now
- ✓ Advances the intellectual estate
- ✓ Represents the institution well
- ✓ Contributes something meaningful

## Continuous Improvement

Every publication teaches the institution something.

After every major publication:

1. Conduct an Editorial Reflection
2. Document what improved
3. Identify what should change
4. Note what readers responded to
5. Update Editorial Standards accordingly

The institution should continuously improve its editorial standards.

Future publications should inherit those improvements automatically.

No editorial lesson should be learned twice.
