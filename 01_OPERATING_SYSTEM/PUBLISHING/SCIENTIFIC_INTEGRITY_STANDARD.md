# Scientific Integrity Standard

**Authority:** Editorial Board + Scientific Review

**Applies To:** All publications making claims about science, research, or biological mechanisms

---

## Core Principle

Scientific credibility is non-negotiable.

Claims must be proportional to supporting evidence.

When research is unclear, that uncertainty must be stated.

Neuroscience must never be overstated.

The institution's long-term reputation depends on careful, honest handling of evidence.

---

## The Five Levels of Certainty

All claims must clearly distinguish which level they occupy.

### Level 1: Established Scientific Evidence

Facts that have been extensively researched and are broadly accepted within the scientific community.

**Example:** "The nervous system responds to threat by activating the sympathetic branch."

**How to write:** Use past tense, cite research, present as established understanding.

"Research has clearly shown..."

"The evidence indicates..."

"It is well established that..."

### Level 2: Current Scientific Understanding

Current thinking based on research, but with some remaining questions or nuance.

**Example:** "Current research suggests that vagal tone may influence autonomic regulation."

**How to write:** Use cautious language, cite sources, acknowledge limitations.

"Current understanding suggests..."

"Recent research indicates..."

"Evidence points toward..."

"We now understand..."

### Level 3: Clinical Observation

What clinicians reliably notice in practice, based on cumulative experience but not necessarily research-validated.

**Example:** "Many clinicians observe that patients' nervous systems appear more organized after manual therapy."

**How to write:** Use "observe," "notice," "commonly see," cite experience but not research.

"Clinicians frequently observe..."

"In practice, we notice..."

"Many practitioners report..."

### Level 4: Professional Interpretation

The author's interpretation or theory about what mechanisms might explain observations.

**Example:** "One possible explanation is that improved proprioceptive input reduces threat signaling."

**How to write:** Use explicitly interpretive language. Make clear this is one possible explanation.

"One possible explanation..."

"We might interpret this as..."

"This could suggest..."

"One theory holds that..."

### Level 5: Personal Reflection

The author's own thinking, experience, or perspective on the topic.

**Example:** "Over 20 years of practice, I've come to believe that permission from the clinician fundamentally changes what the patient's nervous system allows."

**How to write:** Use "I," be explicit about this being personal perspective.

"In my experience..."

"I've come to believe..."

"My observation has been..."

---

## Separating Levels in Writing

**Rule:** Every paragraph should make clear which level(s) it occupies.

### Example of Clear Structure

"Research has demonstrated that manual input provides sensory information to the nervous system (Level 1). Current research suggests this input may influence autonomic regulation (Level 2). In practice, many clinicians observe that patients report feeling more organized after treatment (Level 3). One possible explanation is that improved proprioceptive organization allows the nervous system to more accurately assess threat (Level 4). Over many years of practice, I've noticed that when patients feel genuinely safe, the nervous system responds more readily to intervention (Level 5)."

Notice:
- Each sentence identifies its level
- The reader always knows what they're reading
- The argument builds from evidence to interpretation to reflection

### What to Avoid

"The nervous system changes with treatment because touch provides sensory input that reorganizes perception and reduces threat signaling."

This statement jumps between levels without clarity:
- It states as fact (Level 1) something that's only established at Level 2-4
- It doesn't distinguish established from speculative
- A reader might believe this is research-proven when it's mostly interpretation

## Neuroscience Specific Standards

### Permissible Neuroscience Language

**✓ "The nervous system appears to..."**

**✓ "Research suggests that..."**

**✓ "Neurologically, one possible explanation is..."**

**✓ "Current understanding of the vagus nerve indicates..."**

**✓ "Some research points to..."**

### Forbidden Neuroscience Language

**✗ "The nervous system does..."** (too certain)

**✗ "Touch literally changes the brain..."** (overstated)

**✗ "This activates the vagus nerve..."** (too specific for what we know)

**✗ "The brain reorganizes..."** (too certain)

**✗ "This rewires neural pathways..."** (oversimplified)

**✗ "Neuroplasticity allows..."** (neuroscience as magic)

### Neuroscience Red Flags

Pause and revise if you find yourself writing:

- ✗ "The only way... is through..."
- ✗ "This always..."
- ✗ "The brain is..."
- ✗ "Your vagus nerve..."
- ✗ "You need to activate..."
- ✗ "This rewires your nervous system..."
- ✗ "Everyone's nervous system..."

---

## Claim Proportionality

**Rule:** The certainty of your claim must match the strength of supporting evidence.

### Example: Touch and Nervous System

**Weak Evidence:**
❌ "Touch reorganizes the nervous system" (too certain)
✓ "Touch may influence nervous system organization" (proportional)

**Strong Evidence:**
❌ "Touch might affect how the nervous system responds" (too cautious)
✓ "Research demonstrates that manual input provides sensory information to the nervous system" (proportional)

### Evaluating Your Claims

Before finalizing a claim, ask:

1. What's the strongest research evidence for this claim?
2. Is my language proportional to that evidence?
3. Have I hedged excessively or overstated?
4. Would a scientist reading this agree I've represented the evidence accurately?
5. Could a reader mistake interpretation for established fact?

## Citations and References

### When to Cite

**Always cite if:**
- You're making a specific research claim
- You're referencing a particular study
- You're using data or statistics
- You're presenting someone else's theory

**Don't need to cite if:**
- You're stating widely accepted facts
- You're describing common clinical practice
- You're sharing personal observation

### Citation Format

Use the format specified for your publication medium.

Provide enough information for readers to find the source:

- Author(s)
- Title or publication name
- Year
- URL or DOI when possible

### In-Text References

Keep in-text references unobtrusive:

"Research has shown (Author, Year) that..."

or

"Current understanding of the nervous system response (Author et al., 2024) indicates..."

## Uncertainty and Nuance

### When Research Conflicts

When different studies reach different conclusions:

"Some research suggests A, while other studies indicate B. The current understanding remains unclear, which suggests the phenomenon may be context-dependent."

### When You Don't Know

"The precise mechanism remains unclear..."

"Current research hasn't yet determined..."

"This area needs further investigation..."

Admitting uncertainty is stronger than oversimplifying.

### When Experience Differs From Research

"While research suggests X, many clinicians report observing Y. This discrepancy might indicate that..."

Don't pretend research and practice align when they don't.

---

## Review Checklist for Scientific Integrity

Before submitting for scientific review:

- [ ] Every research claim has supporting evidence or caveat
- [ ] Neuroscience language is cautious, not overstated
- [ ] Clinical observations are clearly marked as such, not as research
- [ ] Personal interpretation is explicitly identified
- [ ] Claims are proportional to evidence
- [ ] Uncertainty is acknowledged where it exists
- [ ] Contradictions between research and practice are addressed
- [ ] Citations are complete and accurate
- [ ] No phrase reads as overly certain or dramatic
- [ ] A scientist reading this would find it credible
- [ ] A clinician reading this would find it useful
