# Punctuation Standard

**Authority:** Editorial Board

**Status:** Permanent Institutional Rule

**Most Critical Rule:** EM DASHES ARE PROHIBITED

---

## The Prohibition on Em Dashes

**This is a permanent Finding My Wei editorial standard.**

EM DASHES are not permitted anywhere in Finding My Wei publications.

Not in articles, books, emails, social media, newsletters, presentations, or any official institutional writing.

### Why

Em dashes often signal lazy punctuation. They can mean "insert something here, but I'm not sure what punctuation to use."

Forcing writers to choose between commas, semicolons, or new sentences creates clearer writing.

Commas, semicolons, and periods are more precise.

### How to Replace Em Dashes

**If you've written an em dash, replace it with one of these:**

#### Option 1: Comma
"The nervous system, when threatened, activates the sympathetic branch."

#### Option 2: Semicolon
"The research is clear; clinicians recognize this pattern."

#### Option 3: New Sentence
"The nervous system responds to threat. This activation of the sympathetic branch is protective."

#### Option 4: Parentheses (rare)
"The nervous system (particularly the sympathetic branch) responds to threat."

## Quotation Marks

### Smart Quotes vs. Straight Quotes

**Use:** Smart quotes (curly quotes)
"Like this"

**Never:** Straight quotes
"Never like this"

Check your editor or word processor settings to enable smart quotes.

### Quotes Within Quotes

Use single quotes inside double quotes:

He said, "I believe that's what they call the 'window of tolerance.'"

### Placement With Other Punctuation

**Periods and commas go inside the closing quote:**
"The research is clear," she said.
He called it "remarkable."

**Semicolons and colons go outside the closing quote:**
The article noted "evidence of consistent patterns"; the clinicians recognized the observation.
He listed three principles: "observation," "safety," and "presence."

### Block Quotations

Use block quotes for longer passages (3+ lines):

> "This is a block quotation. It appears on its own, indented, and represents an extended passage worth highlighting. The closing punctuation goes inside the quotation mark."

## Apostrophes

### Smart Apostrophes

Use smart apostrophes (curly):

It's a beautiful clinic. ✓

Don't use straight apostrophes:

It's a beautiful clinic. ✗

### Contractions

Common contractions:
- it's (it is)
- don't (do not)
- shouldn't (should not)
- won't (will not)
- can't (cannot)
- you're (you are)
- they're (they are)
- clinician's (clinician is / clinician possessive)

### Possessives

**Singular possessive (add 's):**
- The clinic's location
- The therapist's approach
- James's observation

**Plural possessive (add s'):**
- The clinicians' observations (multiple clinicians)
- The therapists' techniques (multiple therapists)

**Plural with s (add 's):**
- The analysis's result
- The crisis's onset

## The Oxford Comma

Always use the Oxford comma (the comma before "and" in a series).

**Correct:**
The nervous system, the musculature, and the psychology all interact.

**Incorrect:**
The nervous system, the musculature and the psychology all interact.

The Oxford comma prevents ambiguity and improves clarity.

---

## Semicolons

Use semicolons to:

### Connect Related Independent Clauses

"The research is clear; clinicians recognize this pattern."

Both sides of the semicolon must be complete sentences.

Not: "The research; shows clear patterns." ✗

Correct: "The research is clear; it shows consistent patterns." ✓

### Separate Complex List Items

"The nervous system has three divisions: the sympathetic system, which activates; the parasympathetic system, which calms; and the enteric system, which governs digestion."

---

## Colons

Use colons to:

### Introduce a List or Explanation

"Three principles guide our work: observation, safety, and presence."

"The answer is simple: listen."

### Before a Block Quotation

"The founder writes:

> 'Block quotation here.'"

### Introduce an Explanation

"The reason is clear: relationship precedes technique."

Do NOT use a colon to introduce a simple phrase:

Incorrect: "Remember: safety always matters." (too much punctuation)

Correct: "Remember that safety always matters." (no colon needed)

---

## Hyphens vs. Dashes

### Hyphen (-)
Connects words in compound modifiers:

- well-being
- long-term results
- evidence-based practice
- clinician-patient relationship
- short-term effects

The hyphen has no spaces around it.

### En Dash (–)
Indicates range or connection between two items:

"Pages 45–52"
"2020–2026"

The en dash has no spaces around it.

### Em Dash (—)
**NOT USED IN FINDING MY WEI**

Replace all em dashes with commas, semicolons, or new sentences.

---

## Ellipses

Use ellipses (...) to indicate omitted words in a quotation:

"To observe is to understand... the world reveals itself to careful attention."

Do NOT use ellipses to trail off in regular prose:

Incorrect: "The nervous system responds to threat..."

Correct: "The nervous system responds to threat." (Period, not ellipsis)

---

## Exclamation Points

Avoid excessive exclamation points.

They're rarely needed in institutional writing.

The voice doesn't shout.

Use a period instead:

Incorrect: "This is remarkable!"

Correct: "This is remarkable."

---

## Question Marks

Use question marks appropriately:

### Direct Questions
"What might you notice?"

### Rhetorical Questions
"Why do we assume technique matters more than relationship?"

### Not for Indirect Questions
Incorrect: "She wondered if the patient would recover?"

Correct: "She wondered if the patient would recover."

---

## Parentheses

Use sparingly:

"The nervous system (particularly the sympathetic branch) activates during threat."

Avoid nested parentheses:

Incorrect: "The nervous system (also called the autonomic system (which has three branches)) activates..."

Correct: "The nervous system, also called the autonomic system, activates..."

---

## Commas

### Comma Splice (Incorrect)
Two independent clauses joined by only a comma:

Incorrect: "The research is clear, clinicians recognize this." (comma splice)

Correct: "The research is clear; clinicians recognize this." (semicolon)

Or: "The research is clear. Clinicians recognize this." (period)

### Serial Comma (Oxford Comma)
Always use the comma before "and" in a series:

"Observation, safety, and presence guide our approach."

### Commas in Lists
"The patient reported: tension in the neck, stiffness in the shoulders, and pain in the lower back."

### Introductory Clauses
Use a comma after an introductory clause:

"After reviewing the evidence, we concluded that relationship matters."

---

## Numbers and Numerals

### Spell Out 1-9
"Five patients participated."

### Use Numerals for 10+
"14 studies were reviewed."

### Statistical Information
Always use numerals: "75% of clinicians," "3.5 years"

### Beginning of Sentence
Spell out: "Fifty-three patients participated..." (not "53 patients...")

### Exception: Years
Always numerals: "2026," "1998"

---

## Capitalization and Punctuation

### Headings
Capitalize first, last, and major words:
"The Role of Presence in Clinical Work"

### After Colons
If what follows is a complete sentence, capitalize:

"Here's the principle: Relationship precedes technique."

If it's a fragment or list, don't capitalize:

"Remember these points: safety, presence, and attention."

---

## Typographic Consistency

### Spaces After Periods
Single space, not double space.

### Spaces Around Punctuation
- No spaces around hyphens: well-being ✓
- No spaces around em dashes (which we don't use)
- No spaces inside quotation marks: "text" ✓
- Spaces around semicolons in some contexts: check your style guide

### Line Breaks
Avoid widows (single words at the end of a line) and orphans (single words at the start of a line).

## Questions?

If unsure about punctuation, choose:

1. A clear period creating a new sentence
2. A semicolon connecting related ideas
3. A comma for a pause

These three tools handle 95% of punctuation needs.

All other punctuation is supplementary.
