# Voice Guide

**Authority:** Editorial Board

**Purpose:** Capture the established voice of Finding My Wei and The Tao of Clinical Touch

---

## What the Voice Sounds Like

The voice should sound like an experienced clinician sharing thoughtful observations with respected colleagues.

After a long day in clinic, sitting with someone who truly understands the work.

Not rushing to conclusion.

Not trying to convince or sell.

Simply observing what is real.

Thinking together.

---

## What It Is NOT

### Not Marketing
No language designed to persuade or convert readers.

No urgency.

No artificial scarcity.

No emotional manipulation.

### Not Advertising
No promises of transformation or mastery.

No claims of being "revolutionary" or "game-changing."

No hype.

### Not Social Media
No trend-chasing.

No hashtags or artificial engagement tactics.

No oversimplification for shares.

No urgency manufactured by algorithms.

### Not Motivational Speaking
No exhortation to greatness.

No "you can do it" language.

No artificial enthusiasm.

No empty inspiration.

### Not Academic Posturing
No unnecessary jargon to impress.

No citations for their own sake.

No hiding behind complexity.

No showing off knowledge.

### Not AI-Generated Content
No generic advice.

No obvious patterns or formulas.

No filler.

No sentences that "sound like AI."

---

## What It IS

### Quiet
The voice doesn't shout.

It speaks in a normal, conversational volume.

Confidence doesn't require intensity.

Authority doesn't require loudness.

### Reflective
The voice thinks through ideas aloud.

It doesn't arrive with conclusions fully formed.

It invites readers into the thinking process.

"What if..." questions are common.

### Observant
The voice is based on what is actually seen and heard.

Clinical observation is valued.

Details matter.

Specificity matters.

Vagueness is avoided.

### Clinically Curious
The voice asks questions about why things are the way they are.

It notices patterns.

It wonders about mechanisms.

It respects what it doesn't yet understand.

It's interested, not certain.

### Relationship-Centered
The voice assumes that relationship is fundamental to everything.

Technique is secondary.

Presence is primary.

Connection is assumed to matter.

The human element is always visible.

### Scientifically Honest
The voice respects evidence.

It also respects the limits of evidence.

When research exists, it's cited.

When research is unclear, that's stated.

When personal experience differs from research, that's explored.

### Intellectually Humble
The voice doesn't claim to have all answers.

It recognizes complexity.

It admits uncertainty when it exists.

It's willing to be wrong.

It respects other perspectives.

### Calm
The voice never rushes.

There's space between ideas.

Readers can breathe.

The pacing matches the calm presence being advocated.

### Quietly Confident
The voice knows what it knows.

It doesn't overstate.

It doesn't overapologize.

It speaks from experience without arrogance.

The confidence is in the clarity of observation, not in certainty of conclusions.

### Deeply Human
The voice remembers that real human beings are on both sides of the writing.

It acknowledges the difficulty and complexity of clinical work.

It recognizes that clinicians are doing this work because they care.

It respects the reader's experience and intelligence.

## Voice Characteristics Across Contexts

### In Project Atlas Publications
The voice is slightly more formal and research-based, but still warm.

Scientific rigor is higher.

Citations are more frequent.

The thinking is still conversational, not academic.

### In Campaign Articles
The voice is the same as all other publications.

Campaign articles don't get a "marketing voice."

They should advance thinking, not just drive conversion.

Readers should remember them because they were good.

### In Educational Content
The voice teaches by inviting observation rather than by prescribing.

"What might you notice..." instead of "You should..."

Stories are used to illuminate principles.

Readers arrive at insights rather than being told conclusions.

### In Books and Long-Form Essays
The voice is the same, but with more space for development.

Ideas can unfold more gradually.

Examples can be more detailed.

Nuance can be explored more fully.

## How to Maintain Voice Consistency

### Read Previous Publications
Before writing, read the most recent Finding My Wei publications.

Let the voice sink in.

Notice the rhythm and patterns.

### Read The Tao of Clinical Touch
The Tao represents the most refined expression of the voice.

If unsure about tone or approach, default to what The Tao does.

### Read Aloud
Read drafts aloud before submitting.

Does it sound like a real person talking?

Or does it sound like an AI generating content?

### Check Against These Questions

- Does this sound like someone I respect sharing an observation?
- Would I say this to a colleague I respect?
- Am I trying to convince someone, or help them observe?
- Am I oversimplifying or overstating?
- Is there room for the reader's own interpretation?
- Does this respect reader intelligence?

### Listen to Founder Feedback

If Founder feedback mentions voice issues, pay attention.

Voice consistency is fundamental.

Deviations from the established voice should be rare and intentional.

## Recognizing Voice Drift

The voice is drifting if:

- [ ] Language becomes promotional or persuasive
- [ ] Certainty is overstated ("This always..." "Everyone knows...")
- [ ] Complexity is oversimplified
- [ ] The reader is told what to think instead of invited to observe
- [ ] Urgency or artificial excitement appears
- [ ] Jargon is used to impress rather than clarify
- [ ] Sentences begin with superlatives (groundbreaking, revolutionary, amazing)
- [ ] The writing feels rushed
- [ ] Paragraphs become long and dense
- [ ] The reader doesn't feel respected

If voice drift appears, pause and reread EDITORIAL_STANDARDS.md and VOICE_GUIDE.md.

Revise until the voice returns to consistency.

---

## The Ultimate Test

Does this piece sound like someone I've come to know and trust, sharing observations from their clinical experience?

If yes, the voice is right.

If no, keep revising.
